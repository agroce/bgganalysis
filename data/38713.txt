RATING: 7
Party/cooperative game where each player tries to help his/her teammates to guess the famous character (fiction or real) through different rounds with particular rules each one. This game is perfect for playing in a meeting with many friends that do not usually play and you want to do something easy and fun. One huge advantage of the game is that a specific surface is not required, it can be played around a table full of glasses, bottles, cans... whatever.
I think that this is the perfect party game. There are other games, that are fun, but that require a bit of organization: a table, everybody paying attention to this or that, in here everybody is focused on the game even when they are not playing, because the character, if not guessed, continues in the pile and it will reappear at some point time.
Positive:
1. Easy to explain and play
2. Perfect for big groups
3. It doesn't require any particular conditions (table)
4. Replayable
Negative:
1. Many characters are completely unknown to everybody and they remain on the pile until are guessed by a person that has tried to describe it previously. This usually happens during the first round
2. If there are shy people there may not be comfortable doing the mimic phase

RATING: 8
A great trivia party game. Players divide forty cards amongs themselves, to which another two cards per player are added. Then, they decide whether to use the blue or yellow names and remove two cards from the ones they're dealt. The stack of forty cards that has been formed will then be passed around between the different teams over three rounds.

Each round, players get thirty seconds to make their teammate guess as many characters (ranging from famous moviestars to obscure poets) from the deck as possible. During the first round, players may use as many words as they like and their partner may answer as often as wanted, but neither can skip a card - even if they don't know the character at all. In the second round, only one word may be used, but players (both the one doing the explaining and the one doing the guessing) can skip cards. In the third round, words can no longer be used and the players will need to use gestures and sounds to describe the character on the cards - skipping cards is allowd in this round too.

The good thing is that by the second round, any lack of knowledge about the characters on the cards is no longer a disadvantage. Players will develop their own way of explaining who someone is, which results in some hilarious gestures during the third round. This turns Time's Up into a very accessible party game, in which the players who's most timid at the start often turns into the most creative 'mime' by round three.
