RATING: 6
First, the Down in Flames (DiF) basic game: On the face of it, DiF is an interesting concept.  Each aircraft's card represent its energy and limit how it can react in the current situation.  It is a very elegant system, and I highly praise elegant mechanics.  However, the problem is when you, say, compare a Spitfire with a Fw-190.  These aircraft performed quite differently but play nearly the same in DiF: a Fw-190 trying out turn a Spitfire in real life would have bled off too much speed and probably find itself inside crosshairs, but this is perfectly possible in DiF.  In short, DiF is an abstract game with an air combat theme.  To be fair, no game has simulated air combat realistically without getting as heavy as, say, the Fighting Wings series.

The DiF campaigns are just a sequence of missions with little interaction between them.  As a playtester of a DiF expansion, I learned that the variance of campaign results was huge and they are near impossible to balance.  I suggest using them as a random mission generator rather than playing the campaigns.  Furthermore, the rules for bombers and campaigns are not near as elegant as the basic game and feel like an afterthought.

RATING: 7
This is the first installment in the Down in Flames series of card games that simulate WWII air combat. Each player has a hand of maneuver cards which he uses to improve his position relative to an enemy plane or fire on the enemy. The opponent is allowed to counter the maneuver by playing cards of his own, to which original player can counter back, etc. The last player to play a card determines whether the original action occurs or not. Each plane is rated with horsepower (which indicates how many card you can draw), performance (how many cards to be held), burst (the values of fire cards that can be played), etc. The number of cards in your hand abstractly represents the momentum of each aircraft.

RATING: 6
Just getting started with the Basic game which is almost excessively luck driven (like Scarab Lords, only one game in three is interesting, the others are routs). Hand size may be the real limiting luck-factor as probability will dictate that smaller number of cards will require more luck to win. Horsepower just defines when that luck is required, low values force early risks. Rules make a simple game more complicated than necessary. Weight is for the Campaign game, the slightly generous rating is for the Basic game so far (short playing time allows the higher rating) (may be a 7 as a Campaign). Tactics so far: Start High, dive to get extra cards, stay Low to optimize Horsepower, kill opponents Wingman when neutral, use your Wingman to gain position on opponent Leader and then destroy him and to deplete his hand, always use/discard so the maximum number of cards can be drawn, when no attack options are available change altitude to deny opponent Wingman an attack and discard useless Attack cards to hopefully have either better protection or Attack/Response cards for opponent's and your following turns.

RATING: 7
Rating for Basic Game: A beer and pretzels game of dogfighting WWII planes. Players draft planes of varying points values (depending on capabilities of aircraft). Each airplane has a hand limit, card draw and attack card value which affects what players can do with card. Damage planes have weaker values.

Players then use a hand of cards (attack, manuevere, etc) to to either damage or destroy opposing aircraft over 6 turns. Good fun, plays quickly and has lots of options for team games, controlling multiple elements of fighters, etc.

The luck of the draw is annoying at times but the game plays so quick as to mitigate that anyway. There are some clever mechanics like a wingman having a 'mini-hand' to attack/defend. The simple system elegantly recreates dogfights. Scenarios add historical play value.

OOP and expensive so I advise getting Zero! (very cheap and similar to this game). GMT is GIVING away a copy of Zero! with the purchase of Corsairs and Hellcats.
