RATING: 9
The map and the components of this classic are spectacular. The map is one of the most beautiful in a wargame.
The dimension of fun provided by this game is multiplied if you use the simo-move system.
Each player indicates (secretly) on a sheet of paper where he want to activate his naval and ground units. 
Then - simultaneously - they move their Units/Fleets to their locations on the map.
The fact that there is a low density of units makes this game a more enjoyable and visual experience that other more recent titles from the same company.
Time flies with this one: Around two hours per session.

RATING: 8
Where to begin? This game is older than I am, and yet it feels tense, fresh, and fun after all of these years. The fact it plays in 90 minutes flat probably helps a lot to make it feel so current. Maybe in the heyday of wargaming this was considered a kiddie game because of its short rules, elegant design, and lack of CRT. Thankfully these are again in vogue.
 
This is a low-density game over a wide, wide front with no depth. You can't possibly cover all all the holes, and too often you'll be bluffing. Control over the Great Lakes and the defense of Detroit are both key... but really everywhere is important! 
 
If you've honestly never played a single wargame in your entire life, then start with this. If you've tried something along the way, go straight for Hammer of the Scots and see what block games can really do.

A very nice Columbia block wargame, the merges ease of play, history, and a healthy dose of dice-chucking-luck.  It actually feels a little more open and interesting strategically than Quebec 1759 (which I also enjoy, even in spite of my additional intrigue into 1812).  I have the original Gamma Two edition, which doesn't have different unit types.  Everything is the same and everything hits on '6's and rolls dice for its CV.  Very straightforward and I can see the new edition adding a bit more in terms of depth, but the original approach to the game in 1973 doesn't leave me feeling wanting for more.  It plays in about an hour and has some interesting areas to go.  A great game for those who thought Risk was the only game that exists where you can dice-chuck a war.  1812 has sense and sensibility and still gives us a game that isn't overblown with rules or numbing through rampant chaos and randomness.  There are some component issues with the original edition, but when you're playing a piece of history about history, I'm willing to overlook this stuff.

RATING: 9
Finally back in print! An essential purchase, not just as an introductory war game, but a quick-playing, strategic, easy-to-learn two player board game!

Amazing elegant war game. The rules are actually four pages long; the other four pages are reserved for historical background and cover art.

The game flows very quickly in part because you are only allowed to move units from only one city (naval units excluded). The small board (which features point-to-point movement rather than a hex grid), very low unit density (25 pieces per person) and the optional simultaneous move rules speed up the game even more.

The combat mechanism, from reading the rules, might seem too luck-heavy (most units only hit with a roll of 1 from a d6), the fact that you keep on fighting until someone is eliminated or retreats evens out the odds.

This definitely belongs on every gamer's shelf, especially those who grew up in Southern Ontario or the Midwest US, and those who have visited Fort Malden in Amherstburg, Fort York in Toronto, or Fort George in Niagara-on-the-Lake.  

I own the third edition, with stickers you must apply onto the blocks. The artwork is similar to Columbia Games' newer releases (so they don't look like the minimalistic symbols of the first edition).

RATING: 9
This is a nifty little game, perfect for introducing someone to the joys of wargaming. And repeated play leads to a deeper appreciation of the inner strategies; it's simple... but each turn requires thought. Since the field of battle is so spread out, there are a multitude of small battles rather than the one massive battle that normally occurs in it's sister-game, Napoleon. And forget about retreating, it's much too bloody. The war covers three years and the American player has to act quickly before the the British reinforcements of the final year overwhelm him. The reluctant American militia must pass a die roll in order to cross into Canada and, in the west, he must guard against the Indians allies that the British can bring into the game. As the Americans, I have a hard time defending against a determined thrust south from Montreal during the first year. So I'd much rather play the British. Also, the key to game is the control of the lakes --but since both sides know this, the efforts there often nullify each other. The bottom line to this game is that it is just plain FUN.

RATING: 3
War of 1812 is too random for me, and not very deep for a Columbia Games' block wargame. This may work as a decent intro to block wargames with young children, but even then I'd go with a game that's more fun.

The fact that your starting blocks are random is of course great for speed of setup, but it can set you up for failure. I can sadly not find the fun in this game. Julius Caesar blew me away with how tense it is, and the War of 1812 underwhelmed me completely in this regard.

There are two things that bug me about Columbia's block wargames in general. First, the fact that you need to roll low to hit. Yes, this is minor as all heck, but it just feels wrong to root for low rolls, I cannot help it. 2nd, the combat system seems to reward defensive play, by allowing the defender to always roll first.
