RATING: 7
Solitaire game where the player is defending the Confederate States of America during the US civil war.  Meaty State of Siege (number 5?) game with 8 pages of rules for the basic game, and another six for the advanced game.  Takes the basic Israeli Independence engine and adds a lot of US civil war chrome, such as general swapping, gunboats, forts, ironclads, and assorted other stuff.  The basics are the same, flip a card, advance the Union armies into the South, then try to beat them back.  The decks are now broken into years, so you'll see the Army of the James and Potomac swap positions in '62 for the Peninsula campaign, and so on.  Tough game, and a lot seems to depend on getting Lee to where the action is, and keeping him alive (neither is particularly easy - once he is dismissed you have to randomly draw him to get him back).  So, a bit to learn to get into it, but a rewarding 90 minute solo game once you're there.  The advanced game (grab the merged rulebook - much easier), as well as the expansion kit, adds just a little extra complexity for a much richer game.  This is excellent stuff.

RATING: 9
Very ambitious solitaire recreation of the Civil War. The SOS system is stretched very successfully -- clever game mechanics, great historical modelling, chrome oozes out of every pore. It takes about twice as long to play than most SOS games, but the experience is worth it. There are so many dimensions to Lost Cause -- player skill, not luck, dominates the game. 
Which leads me to my only quibbles: There is one crucial piece of luck in the game -- getting Bobby Lee into the game early. You are a heavy favorite to get Lee in time -- which leads to part 2: With Lee in hand, you are almost certain to win, once you've mastered the game (and stopped trying to "save" the South, just "win" the game). 
However, this is a solitaire game, you can play the game as is, till you "crack" it (immensely enjoyable in its own right), then do one rule change to each (Basic, Advanced) version (less gamey, more realistic) and then continue playing this superb game.

RATING: 4
Update -- Another play in, and I think this one missed the boat. Too many decisions that don't matter, with the resources, DRMs, and fiddly rules. Example: Commerce Raider. A whole new section of rules for a single counter that you will use maybe once per game, for what? A +1 DRM! 

I've won two out of three. Which is NOT to say that the game is easy, but that you can fluke in to easy wins without much skill, because there just are not many choices to make that matter in the game. Winning strategy was to get lucky with Lee coming in to the East early, and spending practically every free action to fight with him. Completely ignore the West and Mississippi. Every major space was taken except for Richmond, and I still achieved a Major Victory.

Still, my conclusion holds, that it's a somewhat enjoyable walk through the Civil War. I learned something from it.

---

This has a lot less decisions than my other experience with a States of Siege game, Levee en Masse. In The Lost Cause, most of your actions are prescribed (i.e., a battle action with a particular army) so you decide nothing. Your "free choice" actions are so unlikely and ineffective that they don't matter much. This is definitely frustrating, when your free choice actions have generally a 1/6 or 1/9 chance of happening, and even if successful have very little effect.

It's a very detailed game, some of which works and some of which doesn't. The programmed Union army actions are cool. But so much of the details are encapsulated in too many DRMs that become impossible to remember -- +1 for this leader, -1 for that space still controlled, +1 because it's Tuesday, and so on. Also, there are many special rules for different Confederate resources, when they all do generally the same thing.

Overall, an interesting walk through the Civil War. But as with so many, it needs another pass by a developer with the pruning shears.

RATING: 4
What bothers me is you have the same year cards each year, the only variable is the order. If there was a bigger year card pool and a certain number of them (at random) were selected, I feel there would be more variance in the game. The Lost Cause to me felt very scripted have the same events occur in every game. There was going to be a Peninsula Campaign each play, an automatic fort siege by the Army of the Tennessee, and the Emancipation Proclamation was going to happen no matter how the war was going. Maybe it could be held till an army was at a certain location or after a battle loss by the Confederate solitaire player. 
The way Confederate leaders were randomly picked for theater at the start I thought was ok but when they were replaced made no sense. As the introduction said the player was Jeff Davis. Why are they drawn then from a cup when replaced? As Jeff Davis one would know who he would want to put in charge of a theater or military commander. I thought Pemberton would be a minor general, I would have used Kirby Smith (3-0 & 3 MC). All the number of leader casualty checks and replace leaders didn't make any sense to me on the event cards. Why is a player mandated to replace a leader? How often would a theater commander be in the line of fire?
To me the game is too scripted and gamey, with some changes it would be a better game, like more event cards to randomly pick from. On the positive side I liked how the Army of the Tennessee can flip from a land based army to a naval one and it was a quick playing one. There is a fair amount of die rolling for those players who don't like a lot of dice rolling.
