RATING: 7
A fun card game.  Players construct machines with the ultimate goal of eliminating other players by reducing their respective parts pile to dust.  Has a fair development cycle since the machines' power increases as the game plays on.  Be sure to shuffle the cards well!

The 2pl game I played was interesting.  Maybe a hair long, but it was fun!  The card names and artwork help provide a nice theme.  There's a good amount of player interaction and lots of give and take, but it works well.  Simple contraptions can be upgraded into mean-spirited harshness.

I'd like to see how it plays with 3 or 4pl.  The parts piles would be *much* smaller (120 cards total, I believe), but that would help balance the additional time for the additional players.  Seems like it might work, though, again, I'd like to see it in action.

Note that the rules are a tiny bit unclear in one or two places.  Since the cards define the gameplay, that's not wholly unexpected.  We just had any/all connected upgrades apply to the base contraption at once.  Will have to check if that's how it's supposed to work.

A fun game.  Not a shining beacon of new shininess, but give it a whirl if you like.

RATING: 7
This is a fun game of building a zaney contraption as a Goblin Bodger. Once a turn your contraption powers on and effects your opponents. A game of direct competition where your goal is to deplete your opponent's parts pile.

Be warned, you end up needing a large space for each player(up to 4) because the machines build out horizontally. We have a normal card table that we play on and it's not uncommon to have to overlap cards(which are standard playing card size), especially if you play with 4 people.

One thing I don't really like about this game is the fact that a lot of the cards have the same effect, even if they are worded a bit differently. This can lead to machines having the same card effect multiple times, but with different cards.

We don't break this out often, but it's always a bit of fun when we do! It's easy to teach others. The most difficult thing people have is understanding each card's effect and what you want in your machine.

While it's a decent 'take that' game, there are a few issues with the game that suppress the rating for me:

1. Playing Time. The back of the box says 20-60 minutes. Yeah right. This game is far too long for what it is. I haven't checked yet, but I will look here to see if anyone has any lists for card removal, as some seem more logical to remove than others.

2. The Expansion. First off, it's called "Infernal Contraption 2" leading the unobservant reader to assume it means a sequel, not an expansion. Plus, it completely feels like the game was designed with the sequel already made, but it was pulled out to milk money. The main box even has a slot for the extra cards with the name and picture on it! Srsly - don't be that blatant about it. 

2b. The game seems to play much better with the expansion, so it probably would have rated lower without it. I don't know if that was the best marketing strategy or not...

These two problems (and yes, I admit #2 is more of a personal gripe) don't detract much, but it is a pretty superficial game with a few overpowered cards. Seems that there could have been better cards made, but I don't think anything else will come of the game unless it's redone by another company, and I don't see that happening.

RATING: 4
Infernal contraption is an engine building card game. The objective of the game is to force your opponent(s) to run out of cards in their supply pile before you do. This mechanic is interesting because on your turn you can play one or more cards from your hand, but for each additional card you play after your first you need to scrap (or discard) a different card from your hand. At the end of your turn, you then draw back up from your supply. Each player needs to balance playing cards and reducing supply size against what their contraption will do to deck the other players.

Your machine has what is referred to as a main line and plugs. When you start the machine, you choose a target opponent and then from left to right (and top to bottom, which can be confusing - more on that later) you read the cards and take the actions they describe, targeting the opponent you initially selected. 

There are five types of cards in the game, and each has different rules about where it can be placed. The rules around what card type can go where and which cards need power are tricky. We played incorrectly several times before carefully reading and getting them right. 
Power supply cards have no direct impact on other players when the machine runs, but they allow other cards to run. Power supply cards must be in the main line. 
Blue rimmed cards (components) can be used in the main line or plugs, but must be connected to a power supply in order to work. So if youâve used a component as a plug, but not into a power supply, it will never do anything. 
Green rimmed (enhancements) enhance components. They can only be used as plugs on components. They have no impact of the component is unpowered.
Brown rimmed cards (consumables) can only be played as plugs into power supplies. They are removed from the game after they trigger.
Red rimmed cards (sabotages) are played on your opponentâs machine, they have some negative impact. They are also removed from the game after they trigger.

As mentioned above, when you start your machine you play from left to right, and at each main line slot, top to bottom. The top to bottom rule is a little unclear. If you have a sabotage card that causes a component attached to it not to trigger plugged into the bottom of a component, playing from top to bottom would cause it to have no impact. We decided to resolve sabotages that impact only the card they are plugged into first.

Infernal contraption involves luck that canât always be overcome with skill. You can end up in a situation where one playerâs machine is huge and cannibalizes playerâs other machines very early in the game, therefore becoming unbalanced quite quickly. It is not a game to be taken too seriously and it's generally fun while youâre playing. Infernal Contraption is best with 3-4 players, as the deck gets spread out more and ends more quickly. With two players it can go on a bit long. Additionally in a two player game, some consumables are unplayable. 
It's a decent game for kids. The text on the cards is a bit small and can be difficult to read, so Iâd agree with the recommended minimum age of 10. Younger kids will need someone to help read the cards and follow rules that tend to be more complicated than one would expect from a game that, at face value, seems like a light, silly card game.

RATING: 6.5
A CCG-like game: you divide the deck amongst the players. Cards are machine parts that you put together to build a machine. On your turn, you add whatever cards you can (first one is free, then must discard a card for each other card played), then operate your machine from left to right (machine is linear, with plugins off the sides; each card does something, generally either give you cards or take cards away from another's draw pile), then draw up to 7 or discard down to 10. If you cannot draw (draw pile exhausted), you lose -- winner is the last player remaining.

The Sabotage expansion adds cards that allow you to mess directly with other players' machines. I've only played the game once, with the expansion.

With all the text to read, our play was (not surprisingly) a bit slow. With repeated play, however, this should play much more quickly -- it's random enough that you shouldn't really need to strategize much. But it's still kind of a fun game!

RATING: 5

Infernal Contraption is an interesting card game, where you are playing cards as pieces in a machine.  You draw cards from your deck, which is your life, and play one for free.  Each additional card requires a card discard.  Then you run your machine against another player and execute all the machine functions left to right.  The goal is to rip as many cards out of everyone else's deck until they die.

I think the game is a clever concept, and mostly I like it.  There are a lot of cards that you have to learn, so my first play was largely just figuring out what cards do.  One big downside is that the game runs a little long for what it is.  One player's turn in particular took about five minutes. Another player and I joined another group's conversation for a few minutes while we waited.  The game can change so much as cards in your hand can be taken from you or switched, that there's not much point planning ahead on most turns.

Once you know the game and the cards, you can probably bang this game out pretty quick.
