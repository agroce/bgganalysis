RATING: 7
This is quite a brain burner for a simple looking game. You start with a 10x10 grid and add 4 factory tiles. The deal is that roads get built to connect the factory tiles with 1 outlet for each factory, per player that they can place once they have "reserved" a sqare with a cube. The kicker is that the roads get built when there's no other way out of a factory, so other tiles must be on the other 3 sides, either blocker tiles or outlet tiles. So you have to picture the completed board and then work back to place what you want, where you want it. The idea is to have your outlet tils closest by road to the factories. There's bedding too, to make it more fun. Oh, and some additional random tiles you can add.

Fresh Fish could be a simple tile laying game with few rules if it weren't for the two requirements that all buildings need a road access and all road tiles must be connected.
The challenge is in understanding the implications of these two rules when erecting buildings and appropriately preparing these builds by claiming the necessary spots before. Fresh Fish is a subtle and abstract game that will please fans of games like Dolmengtter, King of Siam, Winsome train games and the like.
It's major weakness is the rudimentary scoring system. The proposed variant of money counting only as tie breaker almost entirely eliminates the challenge of managing the money supply.

RATING: 8
A placement and auction game where you're reserving land and building retail outlets that you buy in auctions on those spots.  Streets are built automatically based on an AI that will (eventually) connect to all of the outlets.  You want your outlets to connect (via street) to their corresponding supply centers in the shortest distance possible.  Easier said than done as your opponents can work to invalidate your reserve spots or to route the roads away from your supply centers.

A fascinating game with simple mechanics but with  difficult-to-understand placement and street-building rules.  The difficulty could be overcome quickly if someone could just print out a rule set with thorough, well-written examples.

I give Friese a ton of credit for such a cool design but wonder if some of the strategic depth that came from it did not happen by accident.  At times it can be mind-boggling as their are often hard-to-see domino effects after something's been built.

Unusual, nasty mind-bender of a game. It sounds simple: build your outlets as close as possible by road to the appropriate factory. The trick is that players can't* build roads directly: instead, land is expropriated for roads, as necessary, to enforce the guarantees that a) all roads are connected and b) all factories/outlets (and potential factories/outlets) are reachable.

RATING: 4
I've been wanting to try this older Friedemann Friese game for a while now and finally got the chance, but after learning the rules and playing the game, I'm still not sure I understand it, and am definitely sure I couldn't play the game without someone there to help walk me through it. Basically it's a blind bidding auction game combined with a tile placement game where you're trying to bid on tiles so you can add them to the board and build them as close as possible to their matching tile. It would be simple enough if proximity weren't determined by a byzantine road network system (as opposed to actual distance) wherein the roads are not built by the players, but rather by the game, and only when absolutely necessary to keep the road network linked together throughout the board. Every time a tile is placed you need to check to see whether any roads are necessary to avoid splitting the road network up into multiple pieces. It sounds fairly simple, but in practice I found it surprisingly hard to predict when and where roads would be placed. I'm sure this would get easier with experience, but then all you'd be left with is a blind bidding tile placement game that doesn't seem to have much going for it.

RATING: N/A
Wow! What a splendid little game. The rulebook is awful, and two of the player colors are virtually indistinguishable, but the latter problem is easily fixable since all that identifies a player are generic small wooden cubes (just get some in a different color when you have a full compliment of 5 players at the table). 

Fresh Fish is a brain burner with a sizable dose of luck thrown in the mix. Now, this should be an awful combination, but the game mechanics are just so unique and game play is so FUN that it doesn't matter. You may get screwed by luck (and God knows other players are trying as hard as possible to screw you), but the experience is just so tense and engaging, trying to make things work, that you can't be upset. It helps that the game is reasonably short (30mins?).

While not as accessible as Friese's other titles, definitely one of his better work.
