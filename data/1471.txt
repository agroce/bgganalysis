RATING: 6
An odd duck, this one. The core of the game offers some rather interesting dice-drafting decisions depending on the endgame goal you're pursuing (money, monopoly, or ownership). Choosing your own victory condition and the turn-to-turn dice decisions all work well for a reasonably compelling experience. Where the game breaks down is in its roll-and-move mechanism.

Each turn you roll five dice and, after using one compulsory die, you get to choose two actions from the remaining dice. Not all but most of these choices will be to move your own or neutral pieces around the board into positions whereby you can earn money. Thing is, you have to use the exact number of the die. So there's a lot of counting and watching other people count to see which pieces they can move where. The game can degrade into 5+ minute turns as people count out all -- and I mean ALL -- their possible choices then contemplate which ones to choose. It ... felt... ponderous. 

To solve this problem, the game imposes a two-minute time limit. Sounds good, right? But this just means that people count out their options before starting the timer. I was averaging 15+ minutes between turns without the timer and 10 minutes with the timer. Still a slog. The result is the game takes so much longer to play relative to the quantity of interesting decisions it offers. 

As to the gameplay itself, "monopoly with violence" is an apt description. You move your pieces and neutral pieces (the public) into positions that make passive money for you each turn. Then it becomes incumbent on other players to spend their precious actions attacking your positions rather than building up their own. Like in Monopoly, at some point someone will get better rolls and start to run away with it and then it's just a grinding down of everyone else from there. 

Perhaps if turns were truly 2 minutes long and the timer was started as soon as the previous player was paid and things hummed along, it'd reach a level of speed and hilarity that was intended rather being ponderous. After all, the game actually comes with an included squirt gun that you're supposed to shoot each other with. This was one play with a bunch of new players. I'd give it another try to see.

RATING: 8
Like most AH games, Gangsters is not a 'light' game. It comes complete with seven pages of small-font rules (similar in complexity to Merchant of Venus). Plan on spending a few hours playing it. (The box says playing time is 45 minutes per player.) Each player controls a gang during the Roaring 20s. Your gang includes a racketeer (for purchasing property), a thug (for extortions and shootouts), and a vamp (for seduction of rival gang members). The game board looks somewhat like the bird's eye view of properties in Chinatown. Each turn you roll five colored dice and have only two minutes (timer not included) to figure out how best to move your racketeer (green die), thug (black die), vamp (red die), cop (blue die), and public (white die). There are plenty of strategic decisions to be made and the timer keeps play moving right along. You win by controlling ten properties (joints), or controlling all properties of the same color group, or by earning $10,000. With these three victory conditions it is not always clear who is in the lead--a nice feature.

Now for the real surprise. Gangsters comes complete with a squirt gun! Its use is optional, but I highly recommend it to spice up the game. I quote from the rules, "12.8 Crime Does Not Pay: Each time a player loses a gangster strength point in a Shootout, he may be 'shot' with the squirt gun as an added incentive to avoid Gang Wars or Shootouts with the Cops."

Gangsters is for 2-5 players but works best with four. If five play, the fifth player controls the cops (instead of a gang). In all of our 5 player games, the cop never won but sure had fun with the squirt gun!

Just seeing the shock of your fellow gamers when you unveil the squirt gun is worth the purchase price.

The game's mechanics ensure outright warfare is kept to a minimum while also making it absolutely necessary from time to time.
