RATING: 6
There's a bit of fun here, but only a little.  It plays more akin to "Thunderbolt/Apache Ordie/Scheduler" due to the nature of the weapons and crew tracking.  When it finally comes time to push on the target, the results are quite dull and very dicey, with to-hit margins that don't quite make sense for the primary type of hardware being deployed (ie, LGMs).  If there was a relationship between the opposing defender's actions possibly suppressing fire against an attacking AH-64, that would be one thing, but it doesn't play out that way against opponents who can't truly shoot back.  

Playing with individual airframes gives the impression of tactical maneuver and play, and it's just not there.

RATING: 9
This has to be one of my favorite solitaires also. Also designed by DVG for GMT at the time, it came after the original Hornet Leader and has some borrowed mechanisms from that game, but in my opinion much improved and on many levels a very different game and gaming experience. You are still charged with arming a squad of Warhogs & Apache for missions, but there is terrain now and line of sight. The game is 100% campaign, where each mission results will affect later missions. The game flow is really good, time to play is adjustable by the player, so it can be quick or slow. Like Phantom Leader there are lots of meaningful decisions to be made and the game will get progressive harder depending which missions you pick. The missions/targets have a randomize factor to make for reasonably good replayability.

I love it and look forward to a remake by DVG games which I am sure will be coming on heels of the Hornet Leader II.
