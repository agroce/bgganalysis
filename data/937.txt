RATING: 7
Surprisingly subtle perfect information game.  Very obviously not a German game or a game in pursuit of the German design genre.  The game mostly exists in your head in the number space that represents the various companies and their potential futures.  The economic system has strong "waterbed" qualities (push down here, pops up somewhere else) which would seem to make run-away leaders difficult, but in fact almost masks them from view.  Very interactive in this regard.  

This is a timing game: it is all about owning the right shares at the right times.

Appears very replayable, and again in a rather un-German way due to the subtlety of the waterbed qualities rather than the emergent depth of the rules.  

Beware: Tile choice for the track tiles is astoundingly short/tight for yellow, green and russet

The regionals and their minor companies do a lot to balance and extend the base game in interesting and useful fashions.

RATING: 8
Players invest in railway companies, with majority shareholders becoming company directors.  Players alternate with share dealing rounds (buy/sell shares in companies) and operating rounds (running the companies).  Companies develop railway networks on the map of northern England and Scotland, purchase trains, and run those trains over their routes.  Running a train generates income which can be paid out to shareholders (increasing share price) or held by the company for future expansion (decreasing the share price).  Quite a simple rulebook disguises a rather deep and cerebral game.... and 1825 is considered one of the lighter 18xx games.  If you want to dip a toe in the 18xx waters, this is probably a good place to begin.  Can be bolted to 1825 Unit 2 for a larger game.

The modularised version of the venerable and flawed 1829. An unexpanded Unit 1 really only works for 3 players as there are 3 'big' and 3 'small' companies. The rules aren't by any means complete or unambiguous, which is pretty alarming given that this game originates in the 70's. Track tile shortage can be acute here, creating a very different emphasis from other games in the series. Unit 1 has quite a scripted feel as only LSWR and GER shares are issued in parallel, so some Region Kits and Minors are essential to shake things up a bit. Oh, and the SECR really is hopeless, it should have been the sixth company rather than the fifth in the share issue sequence (it was that way round in 1829). Overall this is a useful 'baseline' to the 18xx system and the 3-4 hour playing time is pretty much ideal.
My rating: This Unit:7, With Kits & Minors:8, Combined Units:10.

Very good.  This was the first 18xx I played, so perhaps it is partially out of nostalgia that I quite like this game.  Unit 1 is quite good for 4 players, and with that number it is not unreasonable to see each (or all but one)company get started.  It is  definitely weaker with 5, as really there are only 3 or 4 good companies to be played. Also, make sure that you print company charters out, as they are not included in the game!

The bank has been sitting there with thousands of dollars and suddenly all the companies start paying out dividends.  The bank clears out in 3 ORs or so after that happens.  This really shocked us in one game -- it looked like things were going to get really stagnant and they the game was over.

RATING: 9
Wow, this is stupid tight.  The linear stocks are neat, but I prefer the crazy stock shenanigans of 1856.  Regardless there is action on the stock market and timing is everything as you may not be able to pull your company out quick enough when barreling toward the end of the game.  I feel this market was perfected in 1860, but it works just fine here.  I will say, that I'm glad the market isn't too crazy here because the track building is very stressful.  The scant limit on tiles makes for very tense timing issues; that unless you control two companies to double build with, you can really fall prey to the caprice of others.

There are other 18xx games that focus on track building, but this really pushes it to a whole new level.  Unlike other track based versions, the game feels that it is boiled down into its most concentrated form without any dilution.  It is a crazy brain burner. A real intense ride 'til the end.
