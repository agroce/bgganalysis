RATING: 5
Just as good as the standard version, even for adults. It merely removes things like "Jacques Cousteau" that kids aren't going to understand. If you are thinking about Apples to Apples and have kids, this one is definitely the way to go, and you could even have fun without the kids. There are less cards than in the original game however, so you may not get as many plays of this one before becoming bored with the lack of variety. Review of the original follows:

In 1998, there were basically two types of party game: Trivia or Charades. Trivia rewards players for various types of knowledge outside of the game; Charades can include drawing, acting, singing, etc., but the goal is to get your team to guess a clue. Apples to Apples started a paradigm shift. 

In this game, one player is the judge. The judge flips a green card with an adjective such as "disgusting" written on it. The other players will then each secretly select a red card from their hands with a noun on it, often a proper noun. The selected cards are shuffled so the judge does not know which player gave which card. The judge then looks at them and selects which card he/she feels best fits the adjective on the green card. The player who selected that card gets a point, and someone else becomes the judge. 

It's an easily accessible concept that will quickly appeal to those more of an empathetic mind than a mathematical one. Probably for that reason, the typical strategy gamer may not find much here to like. A player who finds himself annoyed at the judge's choices, rather than amused by them, will not enjoy this experience. Other times, luck of the draw may play a large role. Ultimately, all the players have the same decision to make each turn: which of these cards is best? Sometimes none of them seem good, sometimes many of them do, but long-term planning and clever tactics will not be found in this game.

Apples to Apples single-handedly redefined the party game genre. For that, it rightly deserves its place in board game history. Nevertheless, much like in music, innovation on the part of the creator doesn't always translate to enjoyment on the part of the consumer, and I feel that is the case here.
