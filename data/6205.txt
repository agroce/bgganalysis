As far the EE goes, well, I am not so sure.  Lawrence Hung, with whom I sometimes disagree, has expressed many of my "concerns" in his comments.  Europe Engulfed just strikes me as new version of Avalon Hill's Third Reich, with blocks instead of counters, WRP's instead of BRP's (why re-invent the wheel?  Why not just give credit where credit is due?).  The comments for AH's 3R show many Geeks holding their noses while they trash the game.  Yet, EE is embraced by all.  Hmmmmmm . . . .

And with combat decided by buckets and buckets of dice, why, it's just RISK with blocks instead of wooden cubes.  Blah!

Allow me to quote Mr. Hung:

"Production is handled by WERP ("War Effort Resources Points"), something similar to Third Reich's BRP ("Building Resources Points") whereby players spend them to build up new units and purchase replacements. Naval action is completely left out in Blockskerig version but I doubt the full version can handle it completely afresh. Victory in a campaign game can only be determined by the end of 1945 to see how long the Reich could survive. At a strategic level game like this, it could have been much more tense if a year by year victory conditions could be phased in (like something in the Russian Campaign). Overall, a game which lacks colours and flavours. It does not add motives for players to do things aggressively, other than repeating what we had known about the history. The political considerations also scripted the game to a historical line where a nation cannot declare a war before a certain date. The designer seems to throw in a mix of herbs from the games like Third Reich's ambition, Columbia's blocks, Axis and Allies' dice-rolling and area movement, TSR's Europe Aflame's scale flavour, and boiling them all over in one pot again. The final taste is a rather disappointment from GMT recently, considered the rather well-received card driven series games."

RATING: 9
A comment on the Victory Conditions: It has been suggested that there should be year-on-year sudden-death victory conditions, but this sort of thing is hugely problematic in WWII ETO games. The playing experience is based heavily on the fact that the Germans get to hammer people early but get their comeuppance later when the Soviet and US steamrollers get going. To end a '41 - '45 game early because the Soviets hung on a little better on defense would be somewhat unsatisfying, and it would encourage "victory or bust" hyper-offensive German play more than is reasonable. The Germans can already cause problems by pushing the offensive in Russia so hard in quest for the automatic victory that they simply implode in '42, which is fine for them but unsatisfying for the Allies, as they don't get to play an interesting phase of the game.
---
A routine comment UPDATE:
While I don't play EE as much as I used to, I got a lot of fun hours out of this game, and I remain a big fan ... it's just time-consuming to play. I always prefer to play Summer '41 to the end ('39-'41 is more a matter of technique than skill, and just not all that interesting), but even that's a full day's gaming. Good fun, but a full day.

The game does not cover political events or alternate possible strategy paths, and that's arguably a legitimate weakness. On the other hand, very few games that allow that sort of thing do it very well (EuroFront is the only one I've played). EE is a bit of a railroad job - you're going to go through the historical motions for the most part - but on an operational level there is significant flexibility: you can pick your objectives in Russia, and the Allies have lots of invasion options. The real tough choices though come in how to spend your WERPS - Special Actions, elite units, tanks, infantry, strategic warfare, and fleets all compete for your attention and even at the height of the US's power, there is far more to buy than you can afford. So choices here make a big difference, and are what makes the game compelling I think. Too many of these games have you just buying out what your force pool allows you, with not enough tension in this regard.

There has been some criticism leveled at the game for unrealistically hammering Britain in '40-'42, and I think that's fair ... the U-Boat campaign is brutal, and while the U-Boats did come close to strangling the UK economy for a time (apparently the U-Boats were the only thing that seriously worried Churchill), it was for a very short time, not the extended crippling the subs inflict here. Also, the reversal of the U-Boat threat happened fairly rapidly once long-range aircraft and other better ASW techniques and tactics came on line; in EE it's going to be a protracted struggle. It's OK, I think it falls into the category of nit-pick rather than serious complaint, and the plus side is that it gives the Germans some legitimate options, but I think the subs probably are a bit too hard on the UK in the '40-'41 timeframe. This is somewhat counterbalanced by the fact that the US can bring many more ground forces to the fray in 1942 and 1943 than is at all plausible from a historical perspective, but this is somewhat unsatisfying balance for the Anglophiles amongst us.

Speaking of which, I highly recommend the increasing US production schedule rather than the flat rate. The flat rate production allows the US to field far too many troops too early.

Also, #1 tip for new Axis players: You *must* max out your U-Boats if humanly possible. They are potent weapons against the Western Allies, and without them the British and Americans can bring crushing force to bear against you far too quickly. Trust me, the 8 WERPS/turn through '43 will be money well spent. Neglect the U-Boats at your peril.

RATING: 5
The amount of historical chrome in the rules kept me from seeing, for awhile, that this is indeed Axis & Allies with blocks.  Like Axis & Allies, it is vastly popular among people who value gameplay over historical narrative, and who think strictly in terms of losses/money and not board position.

The heart of the game is economics, by which I mean, the WERP system ensures that everything can be considered in terms of everything else. What matters is the size of your army, nothing else. Any expenses you can avoid as the Germans results in you having a larger army for Barbarossa. While there is a build limit on the number of, say, U-boats or bombers you can build per turn, there is no limit on the number of infantry or tanks you can build per turn.  As a result, savings anywhere can be translated immediately into more and more ground units.  This is huge for the Axis because, unlike the Soviets, their maximum budget is fixed but their expenses are not.  By a certain play style they can minimize expenses and translate that directly into a much larger German army in 1941.

If the Axis does not invade Norway, for example, the losses they don't take, the Special Actions they don't spend, the pricey paratroops they don't buy, the northern garrisons they don't need, the ships they don't build, are directly translatable into WERPs which can be spent on making the German forces as large as possible for Barbarossa.  If the Germans can (and they can) take the northern swamps in Russia, the lend-lease route is cut anyway, and Norway becomes rather irrelevant - plus the Germans are a significant step closer to conquering the USSR by cutting off Leningrad.

Good German play is very streamlined, totally directed at achieving a sufficient mass of force that the USSR can be conquered every game.  No WERPs are wasted on Africa, Norway, grants to Italy, buying GSUs, buying FLAK, buying fighters, buying field forts, or anywhere else that does not result in a very short term return to profitability and a maximum ground force for the invasion of Russia.  This large disincentive for Germany's historical "expenditures" is a major turn-off for me.

In the USSR, the Russians, when confronted with a gigantic bowling ball of a German stack that can be used to move and attack six or seven times a turn using SAs, have as their best option curling up in the southeast, in a Heavy Fort in Stalingrad along with forces in the adjacent hills where tanks don't get their bonus.  If you examine photos of EE games from tournaments etc you will see this position again and again - the Soviet superstack in Stalingrad, the Germans owning virtually the rest of the board except for Grozny and Baku.  (If they get those spaces they will win in short order.)   I played against a number of opponents and saw this every game, instead of a historical give and take on the steppes.  The Soviets might not win by doing this, due to running out of time, but at least they can often avoid outright conquest.

Similarly the Allies, facing ridiculous supply bottlenecks in the Med, as well as the difficulty of invading Sicily, and the strong need to rescue the Soviets, have large incentives to invade France in 1943 and skip Italy altogether.  Experienced Allied players rarely bother with Italy, especially if the Soviets are getting creamed by the German super-army strategy.

So it doesn't play anything like history.  That is not to say it is a bad game, but though you might think it is a historical simulation based on reading the rules, it doesn't play like one.  It plays like a game with tons of dice, a few large areas with no real maneuver, and gigantic stacks of pieces fighting each other.  It plays like Axis & Allies.  For better or worse.

RATING: 9.25
A block game covering the entire European theater?! Of course I love it! This game, after one play, seems to me to be a real winner. I like the way the game plays out in a generally historical manner, with Germany having tons of options early on, but never really being able to dominate. Britain and France have to struggle to hang on, balancing the needs of a strong fleet, ASW assets, air power, and flak. Russia is doing very little for quite a while, but when they come in boy do they come in with the manpower. The game uses "special actions," which each nation buys with resources, to recreate some aspects of WWII combat, i.e. blitzkriegs. I like that mechanic quite a bit. As the war progresses, the Germans go from a large special action advantage to a small one, to a point where the allies have a big advantage. This is a nice representation of the impact of allied industrial and technological might starting out at a low level but eventually reeling the Germans in, much like the peleton chases down break away riders.

The game also rewards historical accuracy. Sure, Germany could invade Spain if it wants, but that's a sure fire way to lose the war. In our one game, the German player waited until 1942 to invade Russia. Sure enough, it wasn't worth the extra build up time, as Russia had that many more expendable infantry units. As in the real things, the German offensive ground to a halt in the Russian winter, and Germany's proudest army was annihilated by a massive Russian assault. I can imagine wanting a game that gives players a little more flexibility, rather than, basically, having to pursue the same objective the Germans actually pursued. But this is supposed to be a simulation, and like the way it keeps players "in the box" of history, more or less.

Another element of the game I really like was how it reflects the different scales of combat on the Eastern and Western fronts. Combat against the Western democracies tended to involve maybe ten units each, maybe as many as 25 dice to be rolled per combat round, per side. Not on the Eastern Front! The final battle we fought before Germany conceded included something like 30 Russian units rolling (really) 158 dice! Epic!

One small gripe I have about the game is the way the ASW / Flak / Sub warfare plays out. I found it was easy for both sides to ignore flak development, for instance. And even maxing out the ASW assets never really took out the German u-boat threat. With more experience and better play, I may find those mechanics more effective and realistic, but in our game only the sub warfare mattered. Even with that gripe, I like the way the game represents those parts of combat. I just didn't get the feel they were as well integrated to the whole as I'd have liked.

All in all, this game deserves more play, for sure.

RATING: 4
A 4 means "could be talked into it on occasion". I've played this once (3-player) and am willing to give it another go before solidifying my opinion.

Everyone says the Special Actions are a brilliant idea and that is true.

For the most part we all liked the level of abstraction this provided.

Here's my list of complaints while they are fresh in my head:

- No way to keep track of WERPs. This makes Production tedious and error prone. (All the units in Western Europe meant we often overlooked the WERPs for the Netherlands and Belgium.) Simply ridiculous that there is no solution for this in the game but they included a sixth German Special Action chit, just in case you are playing with the optional rule that lets the German have a 6th Special Action once per game.

- Many areas on the board were too small for the stacks that were fighting there.

- Rolling 50+ dice got tedious. Next time we vowed to use the CRT (or at least some kind of iPhone/Android dice roller). Production + all this dice rolling account for the largest chunk of the game time and both feel like something that could be streamlined.

- Not having a weather/terrain effects chart on the board resulted in constant "what does mud do again? And light mud?" every turn as everyone tries to decide what to plan.

- Lots of special cases that railroad the game into historical determinism. In our game Germany was collapsing but Italy was still virtually unscathed. The German player wanted to move an Italian unit up north one province to help stop the Western Allies. "Nope, not allowed in the rules. Just because." "Can't declare war on Vichy until some Magic Date. Just because." "British bombers suddenly become better (even though the player never spent any points on them in the actual game). Just because." Of course the problem is how do to solve those problems (given the current Europe Engulfed framework) without adding rules complexity and play length?

Overall, I'm not sold on the length of the game warranting what I get out of it. (I'm not certain that the extra depth over Axis & Allies is warranted by the longer playing time.) That's a bit of a snap judgment (since it is based on a single play) and probably aficionados will tell me how wrong I am. The problem is, with a game that takes 12+ hours how many plays do I need before that depth becomes apparent? How often can I arrange an entire game for ETO gaming?

(It probably doesn't help that I'm increasingly convinced that WW2 ETO is a terrible choice for a gaming subject, no matter how brilliant the designer. Maybe Bowen Simmon's Stavka will change my mind?)

RATING: 8
This game is exactly what it looks like: an unmerciful, brutal, ponderous simulation of the European theater in World War II. It relies heavily on dice, every rule seems to have an exception, and it railroads you at nearly every step.

But it's a LOT of fun. There are a few things that make this game miles ahead of a game like Axis & Allies. One is the victory condition. Since Germany is virtually guaranteed to fail to take Moscow and London, it only has to survive to 1945 to win. The game is one of slow attrition, of money and soldiers both. The Soviets stand off to one side while France gets obliterated, accumulating infantry and a little armor. (After it actually enters the war, it becomes MUCH more powerful.) France provides a perhaps unrealistically stout defense to the German invasion. Britain has a powerful fleet; unfortunately, its economy is open to being destroyed by German subs. (I still await a a WWII sim with viable naval AND ground-based rules - those here are a bit abstract, and don't reflect the dangers that U-boats posed to soldiers and not just, say, grain shipments from America.)

Once America enters the war, which only happens rather late, it becomes incumbent upon the Germans to stage a careful retreat. It only has to own a couple territories past the end of the actual war to win. I suspect that the Allies win the game most of the time, but since you don't have to actually hang onto the Axis's opponents' capitals, it's not impossible.

Altogether, a great game. It does take an unbelievably long time, but personally, I think it's worth it to have the more granular system than A&A provides.

Although it does some things reasonably well, I remain very unconvinced by EE. My main quibbles at this stage, having played it twice, are:

* I am not sure that the blocks bring a great deal to the party.

* There is an awful lot of dice rolling, which similarly seems to add little.

* The strategic warfare feels all wrong. The Germans build masses of subs, which are low cost/high effect, and there is no political or other downside to their doing so. The subs more or less destroy British production in 1940-41 and, I would guess, in 1942 also, since the British can at best advance one ASW step per year whereas sub building is effectively unlimited. And there is a good chance that poor luck will cause the British to miss a step advancement in one or more years. The effect of the subs is that in mid-1941 the Axis are churning out 50+ build points, whereas the British are typically on about 10 points. As a result, the British are hard-pressed to do anything at all, and cannot even afford naval engagements, for the Axis can easily outpace them in naval builds; and building fighters and bombers is an impossible dream. It seems ludicrous that the British should be on 20% of Axis production capability - in reality the subs were a menace but did not have anything like this level of impact, certainly not on military production.

* The desert campaign is very dull, and feels more like the Western Front of WW1 than the actual campaign, as both sides immediately develop trench lines and sit facing each other. 

* The rules are headache-inducing and abysmally organised. This is made worse by the index which is over-concise and incomplete. Trying to find where Bulgarian units may go by looking up Axis minors, say, in the index gives you the choice (from memory) of 3.11, 5.72, 9.11, 9.23, 11.5 and 23.42, with no indication of which rule might be relevant to the point. After a while this becomes maddening.

* It is absolutely pathetic that GMT 1) did not any include any markers for national production; and 2) that the table only runs up to 30, when production levels and strategic warfare markers frequently exceed this number.

RATING: 10
I haven't played this more than a half dozen or so times, but that equates to about 100-120 hours. The game is a bit railroad-y insofar as you're going to follow historical options fairly closely. 

That being said, while your freedom is limited in the larger scheme of things, and you can't devote resources to political goals like getting the USA into the war sooner or having the USSR attack Germany, you have a fairly large amount of leeway on a smaller scale. You get a lot of freedom to invade Russia how you like, and you can launch D-Day anywhere there's a beach for you. 

In addition, both players will get a fair amount of time to be on the offensive, with Germany getting their doomed invasion of Russia for 2-3 years before Russia turns the tide. 

I have only 2 quibbles. First, fairly important events can hinge on single die rolls--weather and U-Boat attack damage, for example. Second, I've never really seen the aerial Battle of Britain pay off for Germany. If Germany invests heavily into U-Boats, Britain is going to be starved enough economically that the lack of aerial bombing will be a drop in the bucket. I've played this game as Britain/US, Germany and Russia now, and I have yet to see Germany invest in bombing unless they literally have no other spending options left.

I highly recommend starting with the 1941 invasion of the USSR. The 1939 scenario is basically Germany kicking ass on their own for 4+ hours. The Allies could probably start and finish their own separate game night before needing to make any decisions.
