RATING: 7
Pandemic: Streetfighter?  Well, kind of.  As in Pandemic, the Revolutionary players are individual characters, using point to point movement to rush from crisis to crisis - events rather than outbreaks of disease in this case.  And as in Pandemic, cards drive the game;  the events are cards that relate to a specific location (or anywhere) and the players have hands of cards showing different resources that will be needed to resolve those events. With a touch of Twilight Struggle, there are also headline cards that offer text effects or command points. Some benefit the Revolutionaries, some benefit the Soviets.  

As befits a game about an uprising and the attempts to crush it, Days of Ire adds some elements you don't see in those other games, such as snipers, barricades, Molotov cocktails and tanks.  The player characters are best seen as leaders of the revolt and, as such, they can activate lower level fighters, who bring special abilities...while they live.  The main function of those fighters is to throw themselves in front of bullets and shells intended for the characters.    

I am ambivalent about fully cooperative games and so have only played Days of Ire in its one (the Soviets) against the rest version.   As one might expect of a role fulfilled by a game system in the fully coop version, playing the Soviets is relatively straightforward.  Tanks are best massed in defence of events, leaving the State Protection Authority militia and their snipers to do most of the attacking.   The headline cards prompt your trickiest decisions.  The Revolutionaries have  to think about which events to prioritise at what risk of taking injuries, when to spend an action on first aid (four injuries kill a character and lose their side the game), and how much effort to put into activating fighters and destroying tanks.  They have a greater choice of actions than in Pandemic (7 v 4), reflecting a slightly more complex game.   

I suppose I was hoping for a game that was less like Pandemic: Street Fighter, in which the history was more strongly to the fore.  Nonetheless, I have found Days of Ire engaging and enjoyable to play.

The cards are very good quality and everything else is well made.  The board makes nice use of period photos and I found the other artwork appealingly evocative of the time.  The rules have the occasional curious turn of phrase, but are otherwise an easy read.  I found myself referencing them a little too often to check on card text that was vague or ambiguous.

RATING: 7
I've only played the coop rules so far, but I am generally impressed with the mechanics of the game. My concern is the balance (at least in coop mode). More on that below. 

The game basically pulls you in two different directions. First, there's the imperative of getting the right symbols to the right event cards on the board to eliminate them. Most symbols are on the cards in your hand. However, one of your action choices is to "activate" a fighter chit on the board. This removes his/her inactive token and makes their symbols available to you (so you can save on cards). They can even follow you around the board and some offer abilities or actions to you.

So you're naturally going to first look at the board as a big action puzzle. You need to move around the board (you get 1 free movement a turn but otherwise have to discard cards -- ouch!) and take your activated fighters with you so you can resolve events as mentioned above. 

But the other direction you're pulled in is avoiding enemy attacks. There are chits on the board that represent enemy militia, snipers, and tanks. They have their own movement and attack rules, and they activate after you've taken your actions (well, tanks sit there but get to attack you if you try to do something in their space). 

Point is, after you've figured out how to efficiently assemble the symbols you need, you may have left yourself vulnerable to enemy attacks. You'll roll some dice and take 0-2 hits. After a certain number of hits by any one of your revolutionaries, the game is over. Of course, there are healing options in game.

But wait, we're not done. There are two tracks in the game that affect the severity of events as well as how many cards you get to draw each turn. You'll need to put some effort into taking actions that manage these tracks while you're doing everything else I described.

So, it's one big action puzzle of using your cards (symbols and actions) and fighters (symbols and actions) to knock out the events while managing your health and ability to draw cards and withstand the events. You need to last for seven days (rounds) and have 4 or fewer events on the board.

Update: After a few plays, I knocked the rating down a point due to how swingy it can be. The fighters that become available during the game along with the events can be a strong wind in your face or behind your back. That means a session can be thrilling .. or a dull wipeout. I'll have to see how the competitive version plays.

RATING: 8.5
Review of the Hungarian version in Hungarian: below || A magyar változat értékelése lejjebb.

I really like games that can present a not-so-known topic, a historical event etc. to a wider audience, also in a fashionable way (where eg. game mechanics are tied to their theme). I believe this game does the trick: outside of Hungary not so many know about one of the most defining events of our 20th century history, the 1956 revolution. "Days of Ire: Budapest 1956" aims at filling this void with clever mechanics that involve making choices that are especially hard if you give it a thought that 60 years ago an actual crowd of living people went to the streets of Hungarian cities to fight for a cause. 

The game is not easy at all, and this goes to both rulesets. After having played about 3-4 games of each, I think the replayability is quite high. Concerning the gameplay, we could get easier by if we opt for activating fighters in the first few rounds; and this led us feel that, actually, we are not *alone* and are controlling a *crowd* during a revolution.

For me, the most valuable aspect of the rulebook was the detailed historical background of the revolution and its selected events that are portrayed on the cards. My only criticism, however, is also concerning this: It would've been great seeing a bit more history-related content on the event cards to connect the theme more with the game flow during gameplay. Without it, from time to time we felt that the game downgrades to a race collecting different resources to solve events on a board.

To a Hungarian having lived in Poland for a few years, the game's Polish aspects (some event cards, the 4 Polish fighters, consistently using Polish ortography in writing of the names, small visual tweaks like the white-red armband of the guy on the "Aid" card) add a really sweet flavour to the experience.

With a willing group, I think the game will stay on the table for long.


A játék a Budapesten 1956. október 23-29. között történt főbb események segítségével állít pátoszmentes emléket az 1956-os forradalom és szabadságharc történetének. A forradalmároknak a forradalomhoz köthető eseményeket (pl. Sztálin-szobor ledöntése, vörös csillagok leverése az épületekről, sebesültek kórházba szállítása stb.) kell megoldjanak a rendelkezésükre álló hat erőforrás (pl. információ, Molotov-koktél, lőszer, segélycsomag) segítségével.

Mind a "Bajtárs", mind a "Szembenálló" mód többszöri kipróbálásának tapasztalata, hogy a forradalmárok akkor vehetik fel sikeresen a harcot a szovjetekkel és az ÁVH-val szemben, ha köreiket előre, és alaposan tervezik meg -- ehhez viszont egymással szorosan kell együttműködjenek. A játék nehézsége mellett visszatérő érzésünk volt az, hogy mindezek ellenére a forradalmárok megnyerhetik a játékot, s ahogy a körök egymás után következnek, a játékban lévő segítő karakterek bevonásával egyre inkább növekszik az a (")tömeg("), amely a budapesti utcákon októberben ott volt. Ajánlom a játékot azoknak, akik szeretik a történelmi-stratégiai vonalat, kedvelik azokat a játékokat, ahol a dobásos szerencsefaktor alacsony (két olyan akció van, amelyet kockadobással kell eldönteni), s ahol a győzelem előfeltétele a játékosok egymással való együttműködésének mikéntje. Mindezzel együtt bátorítanék mindenkit arra, hogy próbálja ki a játékot a szovjet/ÁVH szemszögéből is -- elsőre picit idegen lesz az érzés, de (bármennyire is furcsán hangozhat) szerintem ez is sokat ad hozzá a játékélményhez.

A komponensek minősége nagyon jó; külön öröm, hogy a három szabálykönyvben a forradalomról szóló szövegek, a kártyákon lévő események leírásai is helyet kaptak. 
Az angol változattal összevetve azonban feltűnő, hogy a magyar kiadásban az 1956-os forradalom lengyel vonatkozásai (Magyarországra küldött segély stb.) nem szerepelnek olyan terjedelemben (kimaradtak a szabálykönyv egyes lengyel vonatkozású részei, az angolban szereplő négy, lengyel nevű felkelő lapkából egy maradt) -- ezért nálam pontlevonás jár, ugyanúgy, ahogy a nem szabványos kártyaméretért.

Ezzel együtt is a tervezők és a magyar kiadó is jó munkát végzett, gratulálok nekik.

RATING: 8
10 plays of the game, in conflict and solitaire modes. The artwork is excellent as is the quality of the components. At first I thought the largely monochrome board was rather dull, but once all the components are on it, then the lack of colour makes sense. The rulebooks - all 3 of them - are well-presented visually and fairly easy to read through and to consult while playing. Unfortunately the text is Hunglish (I assume it was written in English by one of the Hungarian designers). While it is good enough to understand, I think it is a shame that the publisher didn't put much effort into proofreading it. 

The solitaire version of the game is clearly not the main event, but it does work rather well - it is sufficiently challenging and variable from game-to-game to make it worthwhile playing several times. The Conflict game is always enjoyable and is very different each time. The chance factor is relatively high, but the game is fairly short and so I don't mind this. The Soviet player is probably the more challenging role in terms of decision-making (though the game is certainly no Twilight Struggle). But if you play with only 2 revolutionaries each one has enough to do to keep the game interesting for them too. 

Days of Ire has a lot of thematic elements but they tend to get neglected while actually playing the game. The small text for the names of the events doesn't help here, and is a strange design choice as there is enough room on the cards for it to be larger. There is a lot of historical context given in the rule books, but it unsurprisingly presents a very partisan picture, so those who are particularly interested in the history should read a book on the conflict instead. I'm familiar with the history and I don't find the game to be much of a simulation - it doesn't really scratch my itch for a game on this topic. However, I still enjoy it as an innovative and original hybrid - a medium-weight co-operative euro game combined with card mechanisms drawn from the world of CDGs.

Régóta mondogatott ötletem, hogy 1956-ból játékot kellene csinálni, nos megtörtént. A Pesti Srácok az 1956-os forradalom témájára épít rögtön két játékmóddal. Egyrészt egy játékos irányíthatja az ÁVH-t és a szovjet csapatokat a magyar forradalmárokkal szemben, illetve játszható teljesen kooperatívan is a társas csak a játék ellen is. A véleményem most csak a koop variációról szól, amit egyedül próbáltam ki.
A szabály érthetőre sikerült, de azért van egy-két dolog, ami miatt lapozgatnom kellett játék közben is. A játékelemek a magyar kiadásban teljesen rendben vannak, egyedül a kártyaméretért jár a mínusz pont, teljesen egyedi. Nem értem miért nem lehet a standard méretet használni. A játék fix 7 körös, egy-egy kör egy-egy nap eseményét jelképezi. A játék véget érhet hamarabb is. A játékmenet számomra egyértelműen a Ghost Storiest és a BSG-t idézte. Erőforrásokat gyűjtünk kártyák és helyszíneken levő felkelők segítségével, amikkel kríziseket oldunk meg. 11 helyszínen lépkedünk Budapesten és szovjet katonákat és tankokat próbálunk semlegesíteni. A játék normál verzióban szerintem pont elég nehéz, de egy játék után még korai erről véleményt mondani. A sok kártya miatt minden parti mindig más lesz. A játék egyedül is működik, de talán 2-3-an lehet a legjobban élvezni. Negatívumot csak annyit tudok írni, hogy sok újdonságot nem hoz a játék. Elsőre legyen egy nagyon jószívű 7-es, aztán majd több játék után meglátjuk.

[b]--- Summary ---
Solo/Co-op[/b]
Each of 7 days, start with 4 Zhukov actions. The actions are based on a deck of cards you'll cycle through twice each game. Each card has 3 options, one for each phase of the game. Card events either favor the game (Russians) or the players (Hungarians), based on your position on the Support track. Events might place more troops on the board, move the Morale or Support tokens, place more events on board locations, discard events from the deck, etc. 

Then, players take turns in any order. Each gets one free move (spend cards to move more) and 4 (5 in 1-player games) actions (both possibly modified by Zhukov cards). Actions may be used to activate fighters (the board starts with 2 at each location, each of which grants a unique combination of powers and icons), resolve events (spend the required combination of icons using cards and/or active fighters), kill SPA militia or Russian tanks, etc. After player turns, draw cards (# based on morale track) and distribute evenly amongst players.

At the end of each day, SPA militia and snipers move towards players, reinforcements arrive, and SPA attacks (injuring players and/or moving Morale towards Russia). Players win instantly if they manage to eliminate all militia and tanks, or end the game with 4 or fewer events (but those final 4 events are real killers!!). They lose if there are 5+ events at game end, any players are killed, or if the Morale marker is at the bottom of the track.

[b]Conflict[/b]
The Revolutionary side is similar to Co-op, but there's a whole new card drafting system for the Russian player to get and play events and SPA actions against the players.
