RATING: 8
A smarter version of a party game like Cards Against Humanity.  In this one one person is the reader.  They read a card out loud twice that says something along the lines of "Things you shouldn't say during an interview" and then everyone writes down an answer and passes their answer to the reader.  The reader mixes them up, reads all the answers out loud twice and it is up to everyone to remember what was said for answers.  No going back to re-read them.  Then the player to the left of the reader starts by trying to guess who said what.  Once they get one wrong the next player goes.  Each correct answer is a point.  I personally love this as a party game.

A party game that tests your memory and your ability to predict how your friends will answer a question.  It works spectacular with moderately large groups and has been a big success for me with the youth group.  It's not one of my favorite party games, but it's a very good one.  Usually I am not a big fan of games that have no defined ending, but this one seems to work.  You just play the number of rounds you have time for and then the game is over.

What makes the game shine is the openness of the questions.  People are free to write just about anything, and even make jokes based on previous rounds.  The downside to the game is that the memory aspect can be a little too hard with a really large group.  And the bonus for the person who is left standing at the end of the round is a little too high, so it is virtually impossible to win the game without winning a round.

RATING: 6
Things is absolutely hilarious with the right group of people, but it can also fall apart under certain conditions.

Here are the two aspects I don't like about Things:

1) Some players try to game the system by not answering the question. This is good strategy, but it is not much fun. I think the rules to a good party game should motivate the players to do the fun activity. That way people who care about winning are motivated to do that activity that is fun. In Things, players are motivated to be random, so that the active player cannot figure out who wrote their answer.

2) Things can fall apart with a large group of people because it's tough to decide who wrote each of the 10 possible answers. In fact, Things is the only party game I've seen get bogged down with AP on more than one occasion.

RATING: 7
Things... is a party game where everyone writes a response to a particular prompt, such as "Things... you shouldn't put in your mouth," and the players then try to guess which player wrote which response.

A reader is chosen. This reader reads aloud a Topic Card. The players all write a response, fold up the slip of paper and turn it in to the reader, who reads them aloud once and then a second time. The player to the left of the reader has to guess who wrote which response. This is all done from memory, without benefit of taking notes or having the responses read again. If the guesser is correct, he or she continues guessing until incorrect, at which time the player to his/her left tries guessing. Players whose responses were correctly identified are eliminated and cannot make guesses. The round ends when one player has not been matched to a response.

1 point is awarded for each correct guess. 6 points are awarded to the person who successfully avoided detection. The job of reader passes to the left. When everyone has been reader once, the game ends. High score wins.

RATING: 6
Things is a party game.  In it the active player will draw a card and read it to the group.  It may say something like, "Things you wish were taught in school".  Everyone then writes an answer on a piece of paper, and they all give their answer to the active player.  That person then reads all the answers, and everyone excluding the active player, takes turns trying to guess who wrote which answer.  If they guess correctly they get a point, and the person who was matched to their answer is out for that turn.  This goes on until all but one player is eliminated.  Then the person to the left of the active player becomes the new active player, and it all begins again.  The game ends at an agreed upon number of rounds and the winner is the one with the most points.

Things is a fairly decent game for a laugh or two.  It has a few problems though.  The entertainment value of the game is dependent upon the creativity of the players, and their ability to connect with the card that's been read.  Also, the game tends to spiral quickly down to very base humour.  So, if that's not your thing, then you may want to avoid it.  Also, as stated above, players are eliminated from competition each round until the next card is read.  This can make for quite a bit of down time.

There's another party game called Say Anything that has a similar vibe.  However, in it, after the statement is read, everyone writes down their answer, and reveals it immediately.  The active player then secretly chooses the answer they like best.  After that everyone bets on which answer they think the active player picked.  Finally, the active player reveals their choice, and players score points based on whether they bet correctly or not.  No one is eliminated during play.  Due to this twist of betting, and lack of player elimination, I think Say Anything does Things...better.
