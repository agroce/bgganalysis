RATING: 8
(this is the entry for all of my BattleCon stuff unless otherwise noted for differentiation purposes).

An absolutely massive amount of content, and a great game of bluffing/anticipation, chaining actions, and fast action *once players get up to speed*. That caveat is the double edged sword; the first handful of games will be rough purely because folks are new to the system and there are a ton of characters who all have different strengths/weaknesses. Understanding how to read a character from their stats/actions deck is not an easy skill to grok as a new player. The second ding I have against BattleCon is that it's a lifestyle game; it will reward players who spend a massive amount of time playing the game; L99 sort of acknowledges this with league play and that sort of structure. It's not a game for casuals.

The upside is that it's a game that accomplished what I didn't think was possible; taking an experience that is largely split decision and chaotic and translating that into a slower analog medium. Heavy on the employment of Psychology and a mix of strategic/tactical planning, BattleCon brings the feel of a fight and planning moves and doubting what the other player will do to the tabletop realm and the plethora of characters give the game a level of replay-ability that is bang:buck better than most other games on the market today.

RATING: 8
I'm not the type to go weak at the knees over a box full of stuff, but Devastation of Indines is a big box, impressively full to the brim of stuff.  So much stuff that I fear I may never use it all.  

I like the idea of duelling games, but up until now the only one that has stayed in my collection is Knizia's En Garde.  DoI shares the concept of a track with player pieces moving only forward or back.  There is also card play, but in a rather more complex form.  Each character has a small deck of unique "style" cards and a bunch of generic "base" cards.  Each round players simultaneously choose a style and a base card and in combination those cards determine what each character does and to what effect.  The style cards work really well to give each character a distinctive feel.  Choosing a pair of cards offers plenty of possibilities for clever play and for second guessing your opponent.  

The florid sub-manga artwork is not really to my taste.  Card and other component quality is good.  The rules make an admirable attempt at programmed learning, though I did skip the speech bubble rules explanation by cartoon characters and went straight to the regular rules.  Those are clearly written. The suggestions around which characters to play first and card choices in the set-up helpfully get novices into the game with a minimum of fuss.

2P, Easy, Short, Fun

Street fighter the board game. Match a generic base card (dash, jump back, strike, etc) with an attack or move unique to your character. Compare the combined valued on your cards to your opponents, move along a line of 7 spaces, do damage, pick new card pairs and repeat until 1 dies. 
Very simple game but you need to understand your character, and which card pairs to discard at the right time. Otherwise game will be sloppy and boring. Lot of characters. If you know the game and know your opponent and can predict their moves the game becomes extremely fun and competitive.

The only thing they have in common is two fighters fighting in an arena. Everything else is different or the certain parts that are similar this game does them in a different way that doesn't give the console/arcade fighting game feel/style. Now this isn't really a knock against the game. It still is a really good game. I enjoy games that have different characters/teams to chose from that play different from each other and have their own ways of going about winning. I enjoy competitive 1v1 or team vs. team games. I enjoy games that have a high skill ceiling and that the majority of the time the better player will win (playing bo3 also helps this and makes things more enjoyable). I enjoy games with human skills, such as: being able to read the opponent. This game delivers on all of these points. This also goes for War of Indines. 

Having said all that I am super curious to see what heavy online play does to this game. When people that are serious about playing well and winning come into contact with other people doing the same I wonder what will happen with these characters. Once people get to put a lot of time into playing the various mu with a single character I wonder what the mu charts will look like. I wonder what the tier rankings will look like. Will we end up with 10 or so viable characters that if you want to win tournaments you really should be playing one of those characters? I think it will be fascinating to see what happens once competitive players will be able to get in more games more often against other competitive players.

This next point goes some what in hand with the last point. Currently I'm worried about balance. Not because I've discovered any imbalances but because of the extremely large amount of content in the game. Right now there are 48 non promo characters. Each character has a 3 versions of themselves (one for 1v1, 1v2, and 1v3). Each character has 2 super moves to choose from. There are 12 arenas that change up the game. There are 65 strikers to chose from that augment your character. And there's an equipment mode where you can buy gear for your character before the fight. With all that much stuff it would have taken a decade of serious gaming to play test all that stuff. So I would be very surprised if online play doesn't uncover some crazy lopsided set-ups/match-ups.

I based my decision on the value and variability mentioned by fans of the game, and it seemed to be an ultra-powered version of Knizia's modest but clever Duell. And an 8.57 average rating must mean something, right?

Well, I have only played twice so far, once the tutorial game (which we finished off), then the same matchup a second time free-form. It. was. awesome. The tutorial (despite a few apparent math errors) explains a ton of the game in playthrough style, while also answering FAQ style issues. The second game had Kevin up 20-11 on me, before I brought him down to 12. He backed me into a corner, pummeled a few times while I countered once. Kevin up 2-1. Then  dropped my finisher Sheet Lightning for the win.

The game is a Street Fighter type game, and as such is best played quickly in my opinion. I get how AP could KILL this game, so I think that players should have a one-minute sand timer to keep things. After all, a speedy fighting game with the occasional error seems theme appropriate to me.

I've only done the [b]basic[/b] starter matchup so far and I had a blast. I can't believe there are 28 [b]more[/b] fighters in the box. Mind boggling.

So far I've played Eligor against Shekhtur 4 times, and would happily play that matchup at least another 10 times, and likely 20-30 more times. Conservatively, I can get about 10000+ plays out of this... uh... whoa.

I had been hit for 6 and 8 damage by the King character, and lots of smaller hits, and was down to 2 life. This game also handicaps well. One player more experienced than the other? No problem. Have the experienced player take a new character. Or give the new player the "Ex" bases or "Beta" bases for more powerful strikes and higher priority. Or give the new player more life to last longer.

[b]20 plays in:[/b] We are, only now, beginning to use the second set of six fighters. 23 matches in, and we've only used 11 out of 30 characters, and I honestly feel no need to go into the the last three flights (18 characters) for at least another 15 plays. [b]30 plays in:[/b] On my 34th playing of this, I finally played a 3rd Flight Character, which means that, after 34 plays, we've only played with 13 out of 30 fighters. Mind boggling. My initial impression of this game began to take shape well before the first play. Simply put, I began to realize just how much content there is within this particular box especially when you include all of the additional content (i.e., Extended edition, Striker).

As someone whose played their fair share of fighting games in the 90's and 2000's (are we calling those the noughts?), this game is the first to really pay homage to the elements that make competitive play competitive -- the deep need for positional play, pokes, a bit of gut predictive play, knowledge of what your character, and more importantly, your opponent can do. Throw in a few special powers who thematically and mechanically seem to work, and you've got yourself a winner in terms of theme!

Mechanically, this game is a prime example of card play which stands up to the moniker simple but deep. While the framework for this game is robust, it is also relatively straight forward and seems like it won't get "in the way" of the action (once gotten used to). That action is much about deduction, intuition, guessing, and double-guessing what your opponent might do, reacting accordingly.

In terms of aesthetics, this game looks great. Beautiful art work partnered with functional design make this an easy-to-read beauty.
-----------------------------------------------------------
Update (Spetember 2014): I'm finally starting to intuit action-reactions, not in a rich predictive sense (since you can never fully get there), but I'm definitely feeling as though my decisions are much more meaningful (i.e., I'm beginning to make sense of the better action-reaction possibilities and making choices accordingly). Furthermore, I've finally dipped beyond the First Flight -- while it was only one fight, fighting as Voco, I'm definitely getting excited about playing the less straight forward characters (note: my favorite decks in Blue Moon are Aqua, Pillar, and Flit if that tells you anything).

RATING: 9
Battlecon is an excellent game, it really does have the feel of a 2D fighter and the basic concepts of how to play are fast to pick up. I feel that the game is extremely good value for money; you get stupid amounts of contents for 50. 30 characters, a number of bosses, the capacity for 1 on 1, 1 v 2, 2 v 2 and 3 v 1 a battle quest mode, arenas, the list goes on! 

On the face of it the gameplay is very simple, each turn or 'beat' includes all players choosing an attack pair (two cards) and then each character activating in priority order. That's it! Within that though are a multitude of different decisions and powers. This creates an interesting 1 on 1 duel where you're playing cards based on what you have and what you think your opponent will do. What they do can have a huge impact on your activation. If they get a higher priority then they can dodge out of your way, or just get a hit in first, stunning  you, leaving you unable to retaliate. If you can plan for this though you can make sure you have stun guard set up you still take the damage but at least you get to hit back! It is this sort of process that makes the game so interesting and fun. As well as this the different characters feel very different, each having completely different styles and special abilities.

My only complaint is that, especially when you don't know the characters well, the game can really slow down at times while the players choose their attack pair. I've had a 3 bout game last 90 minutes and I think on average it takes 45-60 minutes per 3 bout game. Now that I think of it, this kind of rhythm, a slow build up to a fast exchange of blows, reminds me of kendo (Japanese fencing), a martial art I used to practice. RATING: 8
This game offers no false pretense as to what it is trying to be: a card-driven fighting game. The genius in the design is giving each character a unique set of cards (styles) and combining these with a common card pool (bases) that both duelists have access to to augment their styles. You know what your opponent has, you just do not know of their play timings. The result? A streamlined game of rock-paper-scissors-lizard-spock with an emergent metagame each time you play. What this game sets out to do, somehow distilling the innumerable nuts and bolts of the fighting game experience into cardstock form, it does phenomenally well. 

(+) Despite what seems to be an intimidating rulebook, the game is actually very easy to grasp and start playing.
(+) A breadth of characters are offered to you at your fingertips, a balance between accessible and technical, so it can appeal to all skill levels.
(+) Replay-ability value is near unparalleled for what you pay, compared to typical CCGs/LCGs. Between character mastery, matchup mastery, and the dungeon mode, you have innumerable hours of gameplay...provided you play it.

(-) Requires a competent opponent to enjoy.* A duel is more tense when both players can take full advantage of the part-hidden/part-known information to great effect, i.e. the bluffing and prediction mind-games commence.
(-) High learning curve. Combined with point #1, this results in somewhat of a barrier to entry for new players. The multitude of decisions can lead to some high AP until players get comfortable in the driver's seat of their characters.
(-) Poor component functionality. The insert is absolutely terrible at holding everything after punching, and the cards are not high quality. Penny sleeves suggested (no need to be concerned of heavy sleeve durability, as you do not shuffle the cards).
 

* There is a nice solo variant in the files section that is a true labor of love to the game, and functions well. Not as good as an opponent of course, but it is better than nothing if you are just practicing characters.

RATING: 10
Why it's a 10:

Amazing feel:  I love the diversity of characters, powers, and strategies in this game.  There is a difference between characters in both their special powers and their move sets.  BattleCON feels very much like a fighting game.

Interesting Decisions:  Every beat is a tough decision.  You know the things you would like to do, and you know what your opponent would like to do.  Very rarely is a move a complete surprise.  Moves are selected by an interesting negotiation between players on who will get to make their most optimal move.  This negotiation is not overt: it is a subtle sum of past moves, board position, and previous game meta, coupled with an understanding of your opponent's capabilities.  The psychology that goes into each move is where this game shines. 

Zero sum/quick payoff: Often, it can be difficult to measure how small interactions over the course of the game can result in an advantage for one of the players.  This is because a lot of decisions in games are beneficial in context of the whole remainder of the game.  If I take a single pig in Agricola so my opponent can't get a breeding pair, it is difficult to measure how much food production and points I cost them  BattleCON doesn't have this ambiguity.  If one player's move is good, the other player will suffer an immediate and proportional change in game position.

Does not suffer from the Rich get Richer cycle (Usually): I think it is very important for board games to reward good play, otherwise they are less of a game and more of an exercise in storytelling.  But, a lot of games tend to reward good play by not only increasing one's proximity to a winning score, but also rewarding that player with more scoring potential.  This is an ironic twist where the better decision maker not only gets more efficiency from their moves, they also get game mechanisms which gives them better moves to choose from. BattleCON doesn't generally reward good play with more powerful characters.  Instead, good play lowers the life of your opponent relative to your own or it puts you in a good place to do so on the following turn.  There is some added strength in having a lot more life than an opponent (in that you can take riskier moves with a bigger payoff) but even this is mitigated by a mechanism that becomes active for any player who gets close to losing.  There are characters that bend this rule as their unique features increase or decrease their power over time, but those are rare exceptions (and it is a little more nuanced than that anyway)

This has become my favorite game of all time, of any genre, on any platform.

BattleCON is a fighting game turned tactical tabletop game with near-perfect information that gives you all the time you need to weigh your options and make your moves, yet somehow still captures the invigorating rush of going head-to-head with an opponent in real time.

It's straightforward to learn: most introductory games take 30-40 minutes, in my experience.

It's easy to play: once you know the basic rules, you can pick up any character, read their cards, and start playing that character.

It has tremendous diversity: a cast of dozens of characters, yet you're not required to memorize any of them to know what you're up against, since each comes with a reference card to be given to their opponent.

It has astonishing depth: the number of possible matchups, plus variants that can modify the nature of the battle, mean that no game is truly predictable.

Although designed for two players, it has variants and boss modes which enable practically any number of players, from 2v1 to 4v4 to 1- to 4-player cooperative modes in which the player(s) adventure through dungeons and face challenging bosses. And the lore and flavor of the world are incredible!

While this type of game will not appeal to everyone (unlike Argent: The Consortium, it bears no resemblance to Euro-style games), those who like it at all tend to love it. Download the free Print-and-Play demo and give it a whirl!
