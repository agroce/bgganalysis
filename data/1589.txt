Again, not bad, but would only be truly great at half (or less) of the rules volume.

Circa '85 to '89, this was a huge '10'.

Current rating is based on a been-there-done-that viewpoint, along with feature creep that came later.

My rating is contingent on the house rules listed below.  Without these rules, the rating drops under 5.

Expansions:
Advanced Missions, C1, C2, C3, Y1, Y2, a few Captain's Logs, the strategy manual, and A Call to Arms.

Miniatures:
(1) Romulan War Eagle
(1) Federation Old Light Cruiser

House Rules:

-Allowed rules and ships are contained in the basic, advanced, C1, C2, C3, Y1, Y2, and T modules.  Errata after 1990CY or additional rules must be approved by the house and unanimous player consent PRIOR to the start of the battle.  Absolutely no custom ship designs.

-Absolutely no Worb (Omega quadrant).

-Tournament ships are only allowed if all ships are tournament ships.

-All battles will be Y179 or earlier (i.e. no x-ships, no fast drones, etc.)
 
-Captain's options will only be used to balance a BPV difference between sides; allowed items are t-bombs and slow to medium drone conversions (i.e. no armored drones, no spearfish drones, no SWACS, no MRS, etc.)

-Hidden mine rules are allowed for t-bombs and NSMs dropped out shuttle bays.  All other optional rules are not used.
 
-Non-Hydran fighters are only allowed by unanimous consent of all players.  P/Fs, interceptors, and heavy fighters are not allowed.
 
-Both sides of a free ship selection battle must have an equivalent number of ships unless approved by the house. 

-Excessive use of EW may carry a penalty from The Masters.  Real captains don't need EW.  No scouts, other ships with special sensors, or ECM drones.

-Announcing firing after an opponent announces fire is allowed.  Exact weapons fired should only be disclosed after the opponent has a chance to say they are or are not firing.  EW works similarly - an open auction.

-Ships may not enter a battle cloaked, at zero speed, or moving in reverse unless specified by the house.
 
-The map floats and weapon status is III unless specified otherwise by the house.
 
-Tractoring of a friendly ships is allowed at the discretion of the house.  In a similar vein, excessive mid-turn speed changes may result in penalties from The Masters. 

-Sublight ships are allowed at the discretion of the house.

-No player may play a Federation vessel in back-to-back games, unless their first ship is imploded by an enveloping type-R plasma.

-If the bridge is hit on a Federation vessel, the Captain's skirt is singed on a 1d6=4,5, or 6.

-Don't even suggest The Ships That Never Were or Unusual Ships

-No unplotted mid-turn speed changes (i.e. gaming the impulse chart).

RATING: 7
   A really great game and the one that started it all for the SFB machine, still going strong 35+ years later. Part of the pocket model period of the 70s, I sadly missed out on this at the time because I was too young and have only acquired a copy of the original years later. While this version suffers some from plotted movement and other design artifacts that were thankfully later removed (only launching plasma torpedoes at the end of a turn, for one example) it can be forgiven these faults for the innovative combination of game play elements that truly pioneered a genre. 
   Coupled with the limited Trek license, this game defined what single ship space combat duels could do and no one has topped it yet in that department. This version, like its descendants, does not play as well with fleets as it does for duels. Squadron-sized battles are decent but push the limits of what this game can handle with its high level of detail. While some say this is the best version of SFB due to its simplicity and lack of bloat, I do not agree. The latest Captain's Edition is a well honed set of rules with much of the rough edges sanded away. The tournament game, in particular, is as tight a set of space combat rules with truly limitless tactical possibilities as you will find and may be the perfect starship dueling game. Some, however, find it too limiting and prefer the "historical" game with varieties of ships and expanded rules that the tournament does not include. 
   That said, this version launched the SFB empire and deserves its place in war gaming history.

I've been fascinated with this game since seeing it played in a middle school library in 1979.  The depth of Star Fleet Battles gameplay is legendary, though admittedly so is the depth of its rules.

You can start out with a basic understanding of how ships use energy, move, and fire learned over a half hour or so, then have a perfectly enjoyable game as you learn how each ship's system works one by one. RATING: 5
5 years ago, this game would've rated a 10 from me.  Three years ago, it would've gotten an 8, but I have turned down playing the game to play other things recently.  

I am the author of the "training program" page linked to this review page.

As I've gotten older, I've found fewer and fewer people willing to sit down and spend 5 hours to play out a small squadron action.  It's not a game so much as it's a lifestyle choice.

The core engine is a very good game, and as others have said, it's ultimately customizable.  

I got my start designing boardgames professionally by writing for SFB; I've written or edited or developed several products for SFB, including one coming out next year (C5).  

I used to be heavily into the tourney scene, until I realized that no matter how much I practiced my game, there was a level of skill I'd never reach.

SFB Online is a great addition to this game, especially now that it allows non-tourney play, however, I find that my limited gaming time is going to other projects now.

Top 3 things I love about SFB:

1) The incredible balance between ship types.
2) The tactical richness and depth.  You have a bazillion options when you play, and most matchups have interesting mismatches to exploit.
3) The community of SFB players.

Top 3 things I'd like to change:

1) Remove the tax forms at every turn.
2) Reduce the number of impulses per turn to something more reasonable.  32 impulses of "I go, U go" truly bogs down in a fleet action.
3) Get rid of "rules nitpicks", like changing a stinger's speed from 15 to 14 so that it will move on two consecutive impulses.  Or the dreaded "Turn Break Slowdown".
