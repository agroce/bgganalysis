RATING: 7
Seminal city-building game of negotiation, planning and timing.  Metropolis is one of the few outstanding German games from the early 80s (along with Wildlife Adventure, Niki Lauda's Formel 1, and Kuhhandel), and is the obvious template for both Chinatown and Big City.  Big City stripped out the negotiation elements and focused on the property adjacencies, creating a lighter and more streamlined game.  Chinatown went the opposite direction, focusing solely on the negotiation but throwing out all the interesting and sophisticated parts of the game.  It's amazing to me that Metropolis remains relatively unknown while its shoddy knockoff Chinatown is viewed as some kind of Holy Grail.

Don't build too early!  It will hamper your ability to claim new property, which requires an undeveloped adjacent lot (unless the block has six or more unclaimed lots or no one owns an undeveloped adjacent lot).  On the other hand, if you wait too long, you may find the building supply exhausted.  Quite the quandary!  Also, the big projects almost always give the best returns if they're well located.  Figure out a way to build them, even if it means you have to share the profit with other players.

Trading victory points should not be allowed.  This is implied in the rules by the stated procedure for splitting joint profits - each player earns in proportion to the number of lots contributed.  The rules for claiming property are also a bit murky.  There are no restrictions for claiming property if there are six or more unclaimed (no markers or buildings) lots on the block.  Otherwise, you must own an undeveloped lot (not a building!) adjacent to the property you want to claim, with one exception . . . . If no one owns an adjacent undeveloped lot, anyone may claim the new property regardless of whether the block has crossed the 50%-claimed threshold.

RATING: 4
I'm always up for trying a Sackson game, with some titles aging well and being loved games in my collection. Unfortunately, Metropolis will not be one of them. This is a city-building negotiation game. The board is divided into 80 lots. Players start with a few lots drawn at the beginning of the game and each turn a player takes a new lot from a face up display, builds a building if they want to and can, or negotiates/trades openly with other players in order to exchange lots or share the ownership of constructed buildings. Sounds pretty good, right?

There are two problems that haunt the game. From what I understand, those who play the game today have mitigated these concerns with house-rules. That's great, but I tend to ask "why bother?" One - and this is one I could live with - is that the random assignment of lots at the beginning of the game and what comes up on the draw each turn can have an outsized effect on a player's fortunes. The second is the rule that says when 5 lots are owned in a block, spaces can only be claimed by the players already neighboring them. If the four lots on display can each only be developed by one player and none of them are the active player - then that player is sapped of negotiating leverage and must simply take a card and hand it to the player who can build on it. This creates a situation anathema to a negotiation game! So, pretty disappointing, this one.

RATING: 6
Sid Sackson's games were ahead of their time and most of them are still playable today. This is true for Metropolis as well. It's a city building game driven by negotiation. The rules are simple but provide enough framework for meaningful decisions and trades. Trades are not as varied as in Catan. Less numerous, a single trade will have a larger importance. The luck factor involved in the card draw of plots is acceptable and second to player decisions.

Metropolis seems to have inspired a few other games, most notably Big City and Chinatown. Big City could be seen as a more modern and accessible version due to the abandoment of negotiations and more elements to keep spoiled gamers engaged. I only read about Chinatown whose main improvement apparently are more sophisticated negotiations.

A few rules ambiguities have to be resolved. Specifically, the shared construction of buildings isn't explained well. We decided that we leave a number of chips on the buildings equivalent to the ratio of plots contributed by individual players. In the end, we distributed points with fractions. We also ruled that buildings are also considered to be 'neighbouring' which isn't 100% clear from the rules.

RATING: 6
Another innovative design from Sackson.  It's impressive, considering it came out in 1984 and I'm not only talking about the colour palette.  Simply put players draft cards that provide them with ownership of lots and then can build on the lots (if they own all of the lots required for the building size).  Of course getting lots side by side is not too easy so players negotiate shares in a building, can swap lots, etc.  There are bonuses for location (factory by a river, apartment by the park, hospital downtown, etc.) and penalties (house by a factory, etc.).  Because of all the different building combos and that you're often receiving a fraction of the building's point value there is a lot of number crunching... this could make the game drag so try to keep your calculations to a minimum.

RATING: 8
Mostly rehashing mechanics previously used by Sid Sackson in other games (filling the board by numbers, having cards exposed up for grabs, flexible scoring), Metropolis was nevertheless innovative in its own terms as it incorporated these mechanics in a negotiation game format about building a functional town - Big City and Chinatown, its most obvious offspring, were published more than a decade later.

While there aren't many choices for drawing each turn, and draws can as a result influence the play rather heavily (especially towards the endgame), good risk estimation and negotiation skills can help overcome strokes of bad luck - even for only two players the well-implemented dependencies among the buildings can enable some common ground to attempt deals (and also for some tactical backstabbing). The flow of the game is really smooth and the topic suits the main driving force of the game: cooperation is needed to boost each player's final score.
