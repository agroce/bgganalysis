RATING: 7
The original "crayon rail" game. The map is of North America including the southern strip of Canada and a good portion of Mexico (in the latest edition) and is a grid of dots that are linked using a player coloured dry erasable marker. Also included are goods chips of many types and a deck of delivery cards each having a choice of three delivery orders (take good X from City Y to City Z for a specified bounty in dollars. Several different train type cards that can carry different loads and at different speeds complete the bits.

Players construct their networks by rolling dice and connecting the dots and then moving their pawns (representing your train) along them. It may be necessary to use an opponent's track which comes at a cost. A hand of delivery cards forms the basis for your actions as you plan your routes and string together deliveries. As deliveries are made, the associated card is discarded and a new one drawn. The luck of the draw is very large here along with efficient planning around what you've been given.

Beer and Pretzels rail game. Gateway to more solid stuff...

RATING: 8
I remember playing this back when it first came out, long before the "euro game" craze, and did not realize this was a watershed design. After playing Iron Dragon and loving it, I decided to revisit the original. It is much, much simpler and more forgiving. However, because of slow trains and mediocre contracts, it seems to take a lot longer to get going. Because of this, it has an "epic" feel to it. It is the quintessential "traveling salesman" optimization game, and I like it a lot. Playing with fast train rules (bump up trains to "12" and "16" respectively, and playing to $200M instead of $250M) and mercy rules, a 4 player game can be reliably concluded in 3 hours. A 3P game can finish in 2.5 hours! The short time makes it feasible to play this on a weeknight, after work. Because of this, our play of all crayon-rail games have gone up significantly. It is possible to play to $250M, as this only adds another 30-40 minutes. We have incorporated the fast trains in all of the Empire Builder series, and experienced roughly the same manageable play time. 3 players means more room on the board, and a more leisurely pace, while 4 players gets crowded fast with furious competition. With 5 or 6 players, the downtime is too much, such that the feel of the game play suffers substantially.

It is highly recommended to use this as an introduction to other crayon-rail games; it is easier, more accessible, and more forgiving, with less rules, than the others in the series. Then, moving up to complex titles like Iron Dragon and more brutal maps like Australian Rails is relatively easy.

Empire Builder, a train game before there were "train games". Before 1830 was, Empire Builder am. Before Age Of Steam was, Empire Builder am. Before Winsome was, Empire Builder am. Almost certainly one of the inspirations for Merchant of Venus. Almost certainly one of the inspirations for Roads & Boats. Being behind those two makes it, in my book, a truly pivotal modern game design.

It looks like those old natural resource maps they used to show you in geography class in school with all the cute little pictures in each state to show what "resources" were produced there. Now imagine you took that map and some crayons and some graph paper [all of which are conveniently in your school desk], overlaid the graph paper, and kickstarted your imagination.

This game is too finely grained, takes forever to play, has inane amounts of randomness, has almost no long-term strategy... and is a blast from start to finish. If you think you like Pick-up and Deliver games, you'll positively salivate over this one. "Let's see... get the Bauxite from Memphis, take it to New Orleans, get the Rice, take that to Tampa and get the Fruit... wait, what the hell is Bauxite?"

The quintessential train game.  The most straightforward simulation of real railroading I've played.  It's actually a fairly simple game, just a step up in complexity from Ticket to Ride, but feels like a much more realistic game.  I haven't played any of the 18xx games yet, but I'm guessing most of their added complexity comes in the investment portion--the railroad simulation probably isn't much deeper than this.  

I recommend the public-locking variant, it makes the game more competitive, mitigates the randomness of the demand card draws, and in my opinion adds realism.  We also start with $60 million and 3 build rounds, and use 12/16 trains.

The main problem with this game is downtime, especially with 4 or more players.  Everyone should do as much advance planning as possible, but even so route building can be tedious, and every time a new card comes up it requires some consideration.  So far I haven't found any variants that really address this, and haven't come up with anything myself.  Still a great game though, so worth spending some effort to continue tweaking.

Empire Builder is the first of the crayon-rail games that set a new standard of "train" games and gives players that great "train" game feeling.

Empire Builder uses the tried and true crayon rail building system.  Players use crayons to draw tracks on the board.  Players then use their rails to make deliveries.

Empire Builder uses cards to power a simple pick-up and delivery system.  Players pick-up good from one city then deliver those goods to another city to earn money.  The first to set amount of money wins.

The crayon rail pick-up and delivery system is easy to learn.  New players will quickly learn the game and start drawing their own rails.

Why not longer inventive, the crayon rail system was new for its time.  It is fun to draw on the board.  Sort of like a big kids coloring book.

The artwork is minimal at best, but the components are fine.  Same goes for the theme.  Empire Builder is more focused on mechanics then art and components.

Empire Builder is great teaching game.  This is a wonderful way to learn the geography of the nation.


Pros -
- Tried and true mechanics
- Easy to learn

Cons -
- Games can go a little long
