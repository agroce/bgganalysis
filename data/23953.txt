RATING: 7
Average rating for the OtS of BGG games we're playing.

[b]Royal Rescue[/b] - 7 - really nice wooden logic puzzle game for young kids!
[b]Ladderball[/b] - 7 - fun outdoor game.  Our version folds nice and thin - great to take along on picnics at the park.
[b]Kanoodle[/b] - 7 - very compact solo puzzle game that's great to keep in the purse for kids to play with at a restaurant or anywhere you're stuck waiting.
[b]Block By Block[/b] - 6 - 3D solo puzzle game.  Good to practice spatial relations, especially for kids.  I prefer Kanoodle.
[b]Rush Hour[/b] - 7 - Fun solo puzzle game where you try to free the red car from gridlock.  I enjoyed this more than I thought I would!  6x6 grid, expansions cards/cars available.
[b]Railroad Rush Hour[/b] - 7 - Same idea as rush hour, but on a 7x7 grid with 2x2 square blocks in addition to the 2 and 3 square length train cars.  Also has 2 possible exits that can be opened or closed.  Fun!
[b]Rush Hour Jr.[/b] - 7 - Same game as Rush Hour, also on a 6x6 grid -- just a smaller scale for the game so it is more portable.  Cuter designs for the different cars appeal to kids.  My 5 y.o. enjoys this version.
[b]Pete's Pike[/b] - 7 - Another puzzle game along the lines of Rush Hour, but the playing pieces can move along either the x or the y axis, not just one or the other.  Very nice components, pieces store inside the game board.  I enjoy these puzzlers - very fun!
[b]Strimko[/b] - 8 - puzzles somewhat akin to Soduku, but numbers are grouped in addition to rows and columns.  I prefer this to Soduku. (Plays not recorded)
[b]Washers[/b] - 7 - variation on horseshoes where large metal or heavy plastic washers are thrown at targets (boxes with a wide central ring of PVC pipe).  3 points for a "bullseye", 1 point for inside the box but outside the ring.  Play to 21 points.  Fun to play with the kids, portable for picnics.
[b]Grid Works[/b] - 7 - another ThinkFun solo logic puzzle game.  3 shapes in 3 colors must be arranged on a 3x3 grid according to the rules of each particular puzzle which gives positive and negative clues.  Reminds me a bit of Mosaix which I enjoy.  Good logic puzzle game for kids too.
[b]Top It![/b] - 6 - Solo electronic dexterity game with a beanbag and a handheld device that tells you to "flop it", "flip it" or "top it" - rotating the device several ways as you toss the bean bag into the air and try to catch it again on a different area of the device.  A quick diversion once in awhile.
[b]Raging Rapids[/b] - 5 - I was expecting something along the lines of Rush Hour, but this is basically a jigsaw puzzle along the lines of the "impossible puzzle" series.  Fit the oarsmen into the raft either all facing forward or all facing aft.  That's it -- 2 puzzles.
[b]Hoppers[/b] - 7 - Another solo logic puzzle from Think Fun.  This is a solo peg-jumping puzzle where the object is to hop your frogs, eliminating them one by one until only the red frog remains.  LOVE the container that holds all cards and frogs inside in a nice compact case that'e easy to carry along to play anywhere.
[b]Hot Spot[/b] - 6 - Solo logic puzzle from Think Fun - hop the bots with a goal of getting the red bot to the red corner.  Another good solo logic puzzler, but the pieces aren't quite as nice as some of the others.
[b]Alcatraz[/b] - 8 - Really nice solo logic puzzle with great production values.  The board is a small prison with holes in the floor into which you can insert the pegs of wall segmants.  Move your prisoner through the maze to the exit while avoiding the rotating video cameras manned by the guards (a color wheel rotates between 3 colors).  Has a unique feel to it in that you're not hopping anything or sliding pieces around in the grid - quite fun!
[b]River Crossing[/b] - 6 - Another solo logic puzzle with cool pieces.  Not as compelling as some of the others, but still fun.  The expansion with the dog definitely makes this one more challenging!
[b]Titanic[/b] - 7 - Good solo logic puzzle with a bit of a twist - the spaces to move are waves and the movement rules take a bit more getting used to than others.  Cool theme for boys - my son was immediately drawn to this one.\
[b]Utopia[/b] - 9 - Currently my favorite solo logic puzzle.  Figure out the 4 x 4 grid cityscape using numerical sight-line clues.  The first half plays like soduku (only one of each level building in each row and column), the second half is more of a brain-burner.  This has been great logic practice for my kids.
[b]Mango Tango[/b] - 5.5 - Much too easy for adults, even on the mastery level. Good mathy practice for 6-8 year olds. 
[b]Quadrillion[/b] - 7 - Similar to Kanoodle. Good logic puzzle with Tetris-like pieces. 
[b]Lunar Landing[/b] - 7 - Solo logic puzzle similar to Pete's Pike.

Some of the items that have already been rated here:

Advanced Dungeons & Dragons:  I've been playing AD&D since there was only ONE edition, and I prefer the 2nd edition of the game far more than the 3rd (or 3.5).  The 3rd edition is an ENTIRELY new game, not an updated version of the system.  It shares almost nothing with the first two editions of the game - the way a character's armor class is determined and used, saving throws, and character proficiencies/skills selections and usage/attempts, among other things, are COMPLETELY different in the 3rd (or 3.5) edition than they are in the first two (about the only thing the systems have in common are the use of hit points).  If you learned to play using the first edition rules, you could easily pick up the second and play with no major problems, but to go from the 1st or 2nd eds. to the 3rd takes a bit more effort (it's not impossible, just not as clear-cut.  You would simply be learning an ENTIRELY new game/gaming system).  The same holds true if you're used to the 3rd ed. and are trying to learn the earlier systems - a friend of mine learned to play AD&D 3rd ed, and cannot understand the basics of 2nd ed, although I've tried to explain it to him.  Since the first 3rd ed. adventure I saw happened to be based on the computer game Diablo, and I see many similarities between the computer game and the "newest editions" of the paper-and-pencil RPG, I like to refer to AD&D v3/3.5/4 as "paper-and-pencil Diablo".

My ratings:
AD&D 1st/2nd ed.:  7 or 8
AD&D v3/3.5/4: 4 or 5 (higher with a good - REALLY good - and creative DM)

Sudoku, Rubik's Cube, logic puzzles, other games/puzzles that make you THINK:  definitely more my cup of tea!

My ratings:
8 to 10, depending on the puzzle, and the mood I'm in.

RATING: 7
The DVD Game, "Shout About Movies," is usually found in a DVD case in stores; though I don't think they are sold anymore.  The game is really exciting, becaue there 6-7 different rounds; everything is recorded on the DVD (including the score), too.  You will always have 10 questions in a row, then everyone will have to remember who got what right (never had a problem with this, but I see how others might)

In one of the games you are shown the background of a movie with the characters removed, then later the clothes of the character (but not their faces) are added in.  the first to guess correctly gets the point.  Then 9 other pictures quickly follow.

Other games include guessing the movie on 5 related words, 2 of the games always involve placing a list in chronological order of movies, while another is normally matching the trivia with the screen clip (and one of the trivia statements is a fake)  

The only downside is that once you play it, you can only ever play the game 2 more times (there are only 3 games on each disc).  As if you chose any of the games you have previously played, you will be asked all the same questions again.  I guess you would call this a linear trivia game.  Still worth the $3.00  a piece I got the games for.
