RATING: 10
Another one that sounds pretty fun and casual.  

This is one of my favorite games of all time.  I can't think of a time when I wouldn't want to play it--there's so much fun in throwing dice and shooting the other players. 

Two house rules I use:

Cars are repaired based on their rank, so double 4's can restore the smallest car, double 5's the medium or small, and double 6's any.  

This keeps people in the race a little longer and has each roll, even if all their cars are wrecked, have a chance to bring them back in the game. 

The other rule is the driving through wrecks rule.  

As written, you have a 50% chance of barreling through or becoming a wreck yourself.  I make it so that you only become a wreck on a 1 or 2, to increase the odds people actually try to wreck through.  I've found the 50/50 odds mean people never even attempt.

Milton Bradley's answer to Mad Max. You rocket your team (three cars and a helicopter) down a stretch of post-apocalyptic highway and try to either wipe out the other teams or outdistance them and leave them in the dust. Each turn consists of a dice roll to determine car movement and a combat phase where you try to shoot or ram your opponents. The gameboard consists of two pieces of highway and when the lead car exits the front piece of highway, the back piece is placed in front (after dumping all the wrecks and slowpokes off of it) thus creating a never-ending gameboard.

RATING: 10
Although I really like racing games, Thunder Road doesn't really qualify. It is much more about outlasting everyone else and killing them before they kill you. Driving very fast is one way to do this, but hanging towards the back and ramming is also a good option. 

It comes in a giant box full of classic Milton Bradley plasticky goodness and a couple of boards that are almost certain to be somewhat warped by now. But it looks great on the table. It has a gritty, post-apocalyptic, yet 1980's feel. 

Thunder Road is one of my favorite filler games. It does an excellent job of really telling a compelling story and immersing you in the game despite how fast it plays. It is sure to elicit cheers and moans, yet contains a decent amount of meaningful decisions as well. It's not deep, but it is very thrilling.

NOTE: We do play two minor houserules, which I think improve it significantly.

1. Driving through a wreck only crashes your car on a 1 or 2 instead of 1, 2, or 3 as in the rules.
2. Repair rolls are based on the rank of the car. Rather than only double 6's allowing a repair, double 6's can repair any one of your cars, double 5's can repair your medium or small car, and double 4's can repair your small car only.

RATING: 8.5
At first glance of the cover art, this game screams "Road Warrior: The Boardgame", but it's more than that. It's not some Car War clone, it's a cutthroat race in the post-apocalyptic wastelands where the winner isn't the first across the finish line(none exist in this infinite road), but the last car driving.

The game only took a few minutes to review the rules and get the gist of how to play. Set up is fast and easy to remember. Heck, the setup essentially continues as the game progresses as players leap-frog the two road boards as soon as the leading car passes the edge of the board. Each player has 3 racing vehicles ranging from a dune buggy (cleverly named the "Doom Buggy") to a large tank of car appropriately named "The Eliminator" and all players have an attack chopper that is not a racing piece, but rather a "once-per-track" chance to trip up other players who have a shot to speed ahead and dump the competition, or for revenge.

The track is also littered with wrecks that provide obstacles for any players wanting to use the road for the movement bonus. The wrecks can either be dodged (with practically minimum effort since they only appear on the road) or passed through at one's own risk. If a car is wrecked, it can be saved if you can roll double 6s, which can be so hard that usually wrecks have only 2 chances at being repaired before being dumped and removed from the game.

In all, this game was a great fast paced combat racing game that is easy to pick up and play and replay. If there isn't already some kickstarter to reboot this game then someone reading this, or previous reviews, should definitely start one. They only change that I would recommend for a modern remake of the game is that the new game incorporate dual-sided boards to increase the variation of the race and keep everyone on their toes, even those in the lead position.

RATING: 7
Silly, light Ameritrash fare. Players are trying to eliminate their opponents, Mad Max style.

Roll three D6 and assign movement points to each of their vehicles. Vehicles move along a gridded board and it can either shoot or ram opposing vehicles depending on which space it stops on.

Attacks resolves on a D6. Hits from the chopper and shooting if you roll the value of the car (4, 5 or 6). Otherwise, ramming means each player rolls a D6 and adds value of their car. High roll destroys rammed car.

The selling point is the constantly moving board. Once a car runs off the end of the front board, the rear board is moved and connected to the (now old) front end. Any vehicles remaining are dumped. You can revive a destroyed vehicle if you roll a double six...but if it gets dumped, too bad.

Some flaws: choppers are quite powerful. The game is a huge luck fest. The last player tends to be able to attack more often than not. 


Still, it's fast and silly enough that I'd probably play it if someone suggested it.
Crying out for variants. Must explore those on BGG. Or sell the game, whichever comes first.

RATING: 7
[i]"If I'm gonna die, I'm gonna die historic on the Fury Road!"[/i]

Okay, the trashy classic that is Thunder Road was released almost 30 years before the amazing spectacle that is Mad Max: Fury Road, but the quote applies very well to this game.  (Milton Bradley's 1986 release was an obvious love letter to The Road Warrior and Beyond Thunderdome.)  Each player controls three vehicles and a helicopter racing down a highway trough a post-apocalyptic wasteland.  What is the prize for this race... a tanker of gasoline?  An oasis of water?  Avoiding being pasted across the hot asphalt?  Whatever the prize, there is a sense that your survival depends on you leaving your competitors as mangled wrecks far in your rear-view mirror.  To that end, you must ram, shoot, and speed past the other drivers any which way you can.

The game has a neat mechanic in which the arrow-straight highway and surrounding ditch are portrayed on two separate interconnected boards.  Once the first car drives off the lead board, the back one detaches, dumping any stragglers, and is moved to the front to become the new lead board.  The more cars get dumped, the fewer cars left to reach the end of this infinite highway to nowhere.  The last player who still has cars on the board is the winner.

Great fun with many laughs, cheers, and groans of despair.  This is what simple beer and pretzel games should aspire to.

Thunder Road is Hasbro's answer to Car Wars. You rocket your team (three cars and a helicopter) down a stretch of post-apocalyptic highway and try to either wipe out the other teams or outdistance them and leave them in the dust. Each turn consists of a dice roll to determine car movement and a combat phase where you try to shoot or ram your opponents. The gameboard consists of two pieces of highway and when the lead car exits the front piece of highway, the back piece is placed in front (after dumping all the wrecks and slowpokes off of it) thus creating a never-ending gameboard.
