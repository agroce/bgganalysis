RATING: 7
I used to think of this as Roads and Boats Lite, and to an extent it is.  Many on the 'geek recommend the crayon games as good starter train games, which they are.  But the more I play this, the less "lite" it feels.  It is an easy game in the sense that your task (and the rules) is very straight-forward.  Accomplishing the task well is the key, and it is here that there is much more subtlety than I used to give credit for.  The interaction between the money spent on the network and the distance the train can travel could occasion much analysis, and it certainly is more nuanced than I previously thought.  Having the board be wide open, with the networking possibilities completely open is something I'm really starting to enjoy.  Indeed, of late I've come to appreciate deeply the design styles of the late 80s/early 90s (like Silverton, UvC, etc) that emphasize grinding, repetition, and a longer end view.  The rules are easy to master, and the play is about ramping up well and mastering the game length (which can be long).  This one is underrated.

RATING: 3
For those who aren't familiar with this game system, which is based off of Empire Builder; players build train routes using crayons, drawing track in-between points on a map of the UK. Each city on the map supplies certain types of materials, and demand cards that you have in hand dictate what cities want what items. You control a pawn, which represents your train, and it moves about the board, picking up and dumping off loads. You win the game by connecting the 4 major cities, and having 250 in cash at the end of your turn.

Frankly, I was extremely let down by this game. Turn to turn decisions are straight-forward, and the amount of time it takes to do things is tedious, even with players who play quickly. After each delivery, the hassle of having to locate each city / demand area on the card, cross-referencing that with where you are and where you're going, and cross-referencing THAT with where the goods you need are feels more like busy work than a game.

Luck of the draw is absolutely huge here as well...one player who can get a couple of high value loads to a given city is going to have a huge advantage over the player who ends up with things strewn about the board. The "dump your whole hand" rule seemed to do little to help this, as there were always at least 1 or 2 decent cards in my current grip, and 1 which tended to cause problems, either because of locale or the value of the goods.

On a final note, the opponents in this game should each add about an equal amount of playtime to the game, and I just don't think that they add that much to the system. There is very little (if any) competition for the finite load chips, and on the board, the most they are going to do is make you go a slightly longer way around and spend a few more bucks. I don't think I would even want to spend the time on this solitaire though.

RATING: 6.9
British Rails is a crayon rail game. I had never played a crayon game before and wasn't sure what to expect. It's largely the same as many other rail games except your drawing a path rather than placing hex tiles to mark your path. There's the same type of building cost/terrain penalties that you see in other games. BR is purely a pick-up and delivery route building game.

Your goal is to get 250 million pounds, and build a route to the four major cities. On a turn you move your single train and then either build route or upgrade your train. Trains can be upgraded to move faster or hold an extra good.

You start with 3 demand cards each of which shows three possible deliveries. The demand and production of goods is fixed in every game, so you'll always know that Jute is produced in the far north and will sell well in the south. Trying to jump into a game for the first time is a little rough as you have to constantly refer to where things are produced and whether those goods are widely available or rare. Just finding places was difficult for me. Luckily there were handy cheat sheets or I would have been completely lost.

As soon as you fulfill a demand you discard the card and get a replacement. Trying to work out the puzzle of how to make multiple cards synergize is really the hardest part of this game. You'll want to find a way to combine traveling to a delivery with picking up something you can deliver elsewhere. That's often tricky when you have to factor in 9 possible deliveries along with checking where those goods are produced and comparing their distances to each other and to established routes. After the game I was mentally wiped and had a bit of a headache.

The other big challenge of the game is deciding on routes. While you'll want to build routes to deliveries, you have to think long term about what will be most efficient for future deliveries. In the early game you might spend as much, or more, to build a route as you'll make from your deliveries to that spot, but you're building an infrastructure. Later in the game you'll want to avoid building as much as possible and try to maximize your existing routes. Picking your deliveries will often have to take into account these route considerations... making those decisions all the harder.

I really enjoyed the game even though it was overwhelming. I think it would be a lot easier once I figure out where things are and remember all the resource peculiarities (Sugar is only grown in Lincoln, Fruit is only grown in Hereford, Coal is everywhere, etc.)
