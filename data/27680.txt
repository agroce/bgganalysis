RATING: 8.5
[u]May 2009:[/u]
I really really love this game. Then again, I loved it sigth unseen... So much in fact that I translated the rules in french. I was afraid I might not be able to play as I'm the only spanish speaking player around me.
However I managed to get my girlfriend to play it with me and she liked it too.

I must say the game has not disappointed me. I haven't started deck bulding yet and the game already shines.

I would however suggest playing with the mixed decks for starters to see a little bit of everything.

With 250 cards, I'm actually anxious to try some nice ideas I have.

The game is very balanced, every time I played, it was very close.

The multiple victory conditions all seem viable, though the mixed political + military victory seems easier. Arturo Garcia told me, this was a design choice to force players to play in both fields. It is true that a pure political match up could be a bore (didn't try it though).

If I had 1 piece of criticism (apart from the language dependency): drawing one card a turn doesn't seem like much, specially since half my games, we had all our cards. However, the rulebook clearly states that you can twitch the rules however you lie and even proposes some variants. Maybe try drawing 2 cards or play without shuffling your decks or replenish your hand to the limit, or whatever...
Or maybe the decks we played with weren't optimal and we should have taken more expensive cards so as we couldn't get everything down so easily.
For the language issue: as I said, I played 3 times with my girlfriend who doesn't speak one word of spanish, well clearly, the combat system perturbed her far more than the language. However, I think that for someone familiar with Magic:tg, this won't be a problem at all. It is also fair to assume that spanish would come more easily o a french than to an english (you know, because of latin and stuff!).

However, I really don't think this is insurmountable as the vocabulary is quite easy (I mean, once you know how to say discard, draw and other tenish words that come fairly often, you might have got 90% of the language problem fixed... With the helpsheets here and the game's webpage, this shouldn't be a problem either.)

I rate this game a solid 8
The only reason keeping it from being higher is that I haven't tried deck building yet and this surely is one of its biggest asset.
I'm sure that once I played it more and built my own decks and tried much more, I will upgrade my rating.

[u]December 2010:[/u] Changed my rating system, the game is now rated 8.5

RATING: 8
Rather than a linear reenactment of a war scenario, where two players have to conquer key locations on a map, 1936 Guerra Civil is a more abstract, less fussy but still complex and intriguing distillation of the Spanish Civil War. Nationalists and Republicans fight for political and military goals using surprisingly little material: there's one board where up to two political goals can be achieved by diplomacy, another board for up to two military goals to be won via combat (all the goals are cards that can be chosen by any player during the game, of course), and each player has just one deck of cards built from a reserve plus a board to keep scores.

The pace of the game is dictated exclusively by the nature of the cards, and while this means that bad luck can sometimes stand on the way, there's nothing that a good deck can't make up for (the reserves are balanced, and examples of decks are given in the ruleset for each player to fight with military-oriented, politically-oriented or mixed forces). Due to the small hands used the play is tight enough but not overly so (players can stay a turn just preparing a more powerful move later on) and otherwise very dense: the mobilization and replacement of armies need to be managed carefully so the best goals can be targeted, but politicians are needed as well to boost up the diplomacy and the income; both political and military goals need to be equally guarded as well. 

The historical background is very present in the game as well - not just several mechanisms common in wargames have been translated very well into the elaborate, complex cards, but also the plots and subplots of the conflict. A plethora of archive pictures and factual data just enhance the educational value of this portable, independently published game.
