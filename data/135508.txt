RATING: 7
A game that shows extraordinary fidelity to its theme, a shamanic ritual involving psychoactive substances.  It is fitting that the players are represented by a single pawn on the board, which travels a path through intense moments, chanted mantras and periods of recuperation.  The way that the board unfolds to reveal new sections of the path is brilliant.  Underlying all this are some solid euro-ish mechanisms in which resources (two kinds of energy) are managed, there's a sort of area control, and various things that offer vp's are gathered.  My one real reservation is that the game plays long - both of my sessions have been well over the 90 minutes claimed - and by the end it becomes a bit repetitive.

The artwork is extraordinary, both the 3D stuff on the board and the bad trip images on everything else.  Production quality is good.  The rules are quite hard work and the set-up could have used better illustrations of the components.

RATING: 6
This is the most impressive "art as a game" game that I've played, where the "art" part of the game really tackles the theme well and tosses you into the weirdness of it, and the "game" part is a half way decent game.

Mechanically, players are advancing a shared pawn down a path, where the landing space of the pawn dictates what action everyone will participate in.  Most of the time, the entire group will get to do something, and some times, only one or two players are involved.  Sometimes players will even go on personal journeys, where they now are doing something other than the group.  It's how well you manage those "outside of the group" activities that will dictate if you can score more points than them or not.

Thematically, all sorts of whacked out stuff is going on.  You'll battle the intensity of the trip, awaken to new knowledge, have weird communications, respond to environmental changes, understand mantras, collect paradox, etc.  Oh, and you'll wear 3D glasses for the game, which makes everything in the game trippy looking.  While the theme is pasted on to a basic underlying points scoring system, it's pasted on with really awesome glue.

Overall, it's worth playing to experience for it's weirdness, and maybe I'll pull it out once a year to re-experience it.  But, it is pretty long for what it is, and is "fine" as a game.  The allure is really the subject matter and how the game handles it.

RATING: 10
Mushroom Eaters won't make your head ache from calculations of efficiency but it will leave you howling at the moon!

Mechanically Mushroom Eaters is very simple - play a card move the pawn react to the conditions of the space.  There are subtle strategies at play - what do I need to maintain my fortitude through the upcoming barrage of intense moments, do I play for the greater awareness of the paradox at hand or just be complacent with smaller awakenings- but none to taxing as the game will take you were it wants to.

This last point may make the game sound to on rails for some. But it is exactly this point that makes Mushroom Eaters the brilliance it is.  

Players play nominees for the position of future shaman and at the onset of the game share in a psilocybin laced concoction.  From there players follow a path together, communally sharing experiences as the drug takes hold.  The manner in which you choose to react, how far along the path you choose to venture, all unfold the experience.  

The thin veil of control you are given is thematic genius.  I am not sure people without experience with hallucinogens would quite understand it, but for those of you that have you will quickly understand what is at play here.  You are not the master of the trip - the plants are.  They show you what they want you to see. Can you handle it? Or will you leave a trembling shell of your former self?  

Although the game in a sense plays you - it changes everytime. As close to a psychedelic experience as I can imagine a game becoming.  

Mushroom Eaters is a true work of art that transcends the board and communicates a difficult (misunderstood) subject matter better than most of the other attempts I have encountered in any medium.

Nate Hayden is a true force in modern game design, and Mushroom Eaters continues his amazing lineage.

RATING: 8
Heavy, man.

I really admire the designer for realising such a truly independent and rigorous vision while still succeeding in making a good game. This ignores the hype and commercial momentum of the board game industry and manages to be like nothing else. Deeply thematic, utterly strange, and borderline genius.

I can love an ugly game (I'm looking at you, [thing=118][/thing]), but I strongly appreciate good aesthetics, and this game has an incredible aesthetic. A game about taking a spiritual hallucinogenic trip that is so absorbing and surreal that you actually feel like you've taken one.

The Talking rules are a bit confusing. I'm tempted to go back to the first edition rules for the sake of slightly simplifying Talking (where you give a Talking card to another player, they apply effects, then take another talking card to give to a player who hasn't had one yet, and so on until all players have been "Talked" to - no board involved).

The play time on the box is a lie. This is a 2-3 hour game. In fact, I played it once with 5 players and we had to skip a chunk of the board to keep it to 3 hours.

Crowdfunded in July 2014, received March 2015.

RATING: 6
Man, this is a tricky one.

I'm basically a Eurogamer. On one level, this is a simple game of playing cards to move a shared pawn down a long track, performing the individual or shared action each place you stop. Throw in a few ways to score VP, a few resources to manage, a few tracks to keep topped up, and there you have it. It actually reminded me of Knizia's Beowulf near the end.

In Mushroom Eaters, the game itself is less significant than the theme & artwork. The concept is superb - for once we're dealing with an individual's path to personal enlightenment rather than becoming the best trader, fighter or satisfying some king.

Where I have a problem is that the artwork matches my instinctive (taught?) feelings towards drug use - the game is dark & depressing, almost [i]too[/i] evocative, but becomes great fun if you adopt a stoner hippy / Scooby Doo approach.

I couldn't get my Scooby Doo thing going in the face of so much darkness. So: a game that feels very true to its theme. [i]Too[/i] true to its theme for me.

Having said all that ... I'd like to play it again.

I'd lighten up - and adopt a less risk-averse approach, focusing less on maintaining nervous system energy and more on faces & drums.

Certainly felt like a unique game, though. I can't recall being so emotionally connected with a game before.
